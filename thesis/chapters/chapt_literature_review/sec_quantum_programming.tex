\section{Quantum programming}\label{sec:lit_rev:quantum_programming}

The mathematical and physical foundations of quantum computation are assumed throughout this section; qubits, gates and measurement are established in Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction} and are not revisited here.

This section surveys the abstract models of quantum computation, the programming paradigms built over them and the constraints that recur across quantum languages regardless of the model that underlies them.
The section is concerned with models of computation and programming paradigms, not with compilation pipelines and intermediate representations; those are examined in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:compilation}.

\subsection{Models of quantum computation}\label{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:models_of_computation}

Quantum computation can be described through three families of abstract machine models, each addressing a different question about the nature of computation.
Computation-definition models, including the quantum Turing machine and the quantum lambda calculus, provide formal accounts of what quantum computation is~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines,wang2023qrasp}.
Instruction-execution models, including the quantum random access machine and stored-program variants, specify how it is described and run at the level of an addressable machine~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines,wang2023qrasp}.
Execution models specify how computation is encoded as a process; they define a computational paradigm rather than a hardware substrate.
The circuit, measurement-based (MBQC), adiabatic, quantum walk, quantum cellular automata (QCA) and ancilla-driven (ADQC) models populate this third family.
Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:quantum_programming:execution_model_descriptions} describes each model, Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:quantum_programming:model_mechanisms} provides a graphical interpretation.

All three families achieve universal quantum computation and the execution models are polynomially equivalent to one another~\cite{aharonov2007adiabatic,childs2009quantumwalk,farrelly2020qca}.
They differ not in computational power but in what each encodes into the structure of a well-formed program: the circuit model requires explicit specification of every gate and qubit.
MBQC encodes computation in a resource state and a measurement pattern; QCA encodes it in an initial lattice configuration under a fixed rule.
These differences are not incidental to language design; a programming language built on a given model inherits its structural assumptions and can only hide from the programmer what the model itself makes implicit.

Computation-definition models provide a formal account of what quantum computation is, independent of how a program is described or run.
The quantum Turing machine is the canonical model of this kind, generalising the classical Turing machine so that the transition function assigns a complex amplitude to each move~\cite{bernstein1997quantum}.
Its evolution is required to be unitary.
Where a probabilistic Turing machine keeps its move probabilities summing to one, the quantum machine must evolve so that the squared amplitudes across all configurations continue to sum to one.
This ensures the machine is in a physically realisable state at every step and guaranteeing that measurement yields a genuine outcome~\cite{bernstein1997quantum}.
Reversibility follows from this requirement rather than being imposed separately and definite outcomes arise only when the final superposition is measured~\cite{bernstein1997quantum}.
The quantum lambda calculus reaches an equivalent account through a higher-order, type-theoretic route, characterising quantum computation in terms of functions rather than machine configurations~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.

Well-formedness in these models is a global semantic property rather than a syntactic one.
In the quantum Turing machine, a transition function is legal precisely when the operator it induces over the space of configurations is unitary, a condition that no local rule of grammar can enforce on its own~\cite{bernstein1997quantum}.
This is a constraint on quantum computation itself rather than on any particular machine~\cite{bernstein1997quantum}; the reversibility it imposes is inherited by every execution model and every language constructed above them.
These models therefore fix what a quantum program must satisfy without prescribing how it is encoded or executed, establishing the model-invariant discipline against which the structural commitments of the execution models can be assessed.

Instruction-execution models describe how a quantum program is expressed as a sequence of operations and run on an addressable machine.
The quantum random access machine pairs a classical controller with a quantum register and exposes only three operations on that register: preparing a state, applying a unitary and measuring~\cite{knill1996pseudocode}.
A quantum register is treated as a classical register not known to hold a classical value, so the line between classical and quantum data is drawn by the operations permitted rather than by the storage itself~\cite{knill1996pseudocode}.
Control remains entirely classical, with the controller issuing unitaries and reading results back through measurement while reusing familiar constructs such as assignment and conditional execution~\cite{knill1996pseudocode}.
A formal development of the model adds a finite instruction set over unbounded classical and quantum registers.
Its stored-program variant places the program in those registers with an instruction counter, allowing the machine to modify its own instructions as it runs~\cite{wang2023qrasp}.
Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:quantum_programming:instruction_execution} demonstrates this pattern.
Both machines simulate and are simulated by the quantum Turing machine within polynomial time bounds, adding an apparatus for describing and running programs without changing what can be computed~\cite{wang2023qrasp}.

The structural commitments of these models become visible where control itself is made quantum rather than left to a classical controller.
The quantum control machine replaces the classical instruction cycle with one in which fetching, executing and retiring each instruction are unitary operations over quantum control registers, the program counter among them~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
A classical conditional jump lifted directly into superposition would cancel the interference the computation depends on, so realisable control flow must remain reversible and bring every branch to the same program point at the same step.
Bounded loops are the only quantum loops these conditions admit~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
The unitarity discipline fixed by the computation-definition models thus propagates upward into the instruction set, governing which control constructs an execution model may even offer.

The criteria for a productive quantum abstract machine were established in the assessment of abstraction in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction}, where the conclusion was that no existing machine satisfies the full set and that the requirements for a symbolic instruction architecture are the principal gaps~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
Those conclusions can be tested against the three families described above.
The criteria apply unevenly, since several of them presuppose a model that already claims to be a programmable machine.
The computation-definition models fix what a computation is and provide no instruction set, so the demand for a stable and hardware-independent architecture falls on the instruction-execution family rather than on them.
The quantum random access and stored-program machines in that family are judged to fail the requirement that machine state be finite and addressable in advance, on the grounds that they assume unbounded registers~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
This judgement is too strong.
A stored-program machine occupies only finitely many registers at any step and a time-constructible program can compute its own resource bound from its input with certainty, fixing that bound before it runs~\cite{wang2023qrasp}.
An unbounded register file is in any case the same idealisation made by the classical random access machine, which the same criteria hold up as a model of effective abstraction~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
The requirement that excludes the quantum machines on this basis is better read as a demand for addressable and decomposable state, which the stored-program machine provides.

A second criterion, that instructions be symbolic rather than tied to circuit elements, is met only in part.
The control and memory instructions of these machines, among them load, store, branch and arithmetic, abstract over their realisation much as an addition instruction stands apart from any particular adder~\cite{wang2023qrasp}.
Their quantum instructions do not.
An instruction to apply a Hadamard or Pauli operator names a gate of the circuit model and its meaning is the amplitude transformation that gate performs rather than a higher account of the operation being carried out~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
The separation between instruction symbol and circuit element that the classical instructions achieve is absent for the quantum ones and this, rather than any wholesale dependence on circuits, is what keeps the models reasoning in circuit terms~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.

Control flow varies across these models.
The quantum Turing machine has no native control construct.
A fixed transition rule drives its evolution and the only choice exercised over the computation is the external decision of when to measure~\cite{bernstein1997quantum}.
Its control is classical only by omission, residing outside the quantum state rather than being expressed within the machine.
The quantum control machine takes a different approach, placing the program counter and a branch register into the quantum state so that branching itself can occur in superposition~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
The machine still requires branches to rejoin at a common program point with a fixed running time, so quantum control flow remains bounded by classical control structure rather than replacing it.
The QCM demonstrates that quantum control is achievable within an instruction-execution model, but only under classical bounds; its remaining shortfalls lie in the abstraction of its instructions and in its physical realisability.

The execution models describe how a computation is encoded as a physical process rather than what it computes or how its instructions are sequenced.
The circuit model composes a computation from elementary gates~\cite{yao1993circuit}, the measurement-based model drives it through adaptive measurements on a pre-prepared entangled state~\cite{raussendorf2001oneway}.
The adiabatic model holds the answer in the ground state of a slowly varying Hamiltonian~\cite{aharonov2007adiabatic} and the quantum walk model encodes it in the dynamics of a walker over a graph~\cite{childs2009quantumwalk}.
Each simulates and is simulated by the circuit model within polynomial bounds, so all four characterise the same class of efficiently solvable problems.
Equivalence of this kind settles the question of power; it leaves untouched the question of representation, of how readily a model lets a computation be expressed and reasoned about.
The models are equivalent in power and differ only in how each realises sequencing, the role played by time and it is this difference that governs how readily each can be expressed, as set out in Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:quantum_programming:execution_models}.

On that axis the circuit model is the one whose structure coincides with sequential composition.
Its gates carry fixed and context-independent meanings whose composition is the meaning of the whole, a compositionality the other models recover only under a momentum condition, disrupt with tracked corrections, or replace with a global spectral-gap condition~\cite{childs2009quantumwalk,raussendorf2001oneway,aharonov2007adiabatic}.
Its sequencing is already an explicit ordering of operations, the form a program takes, whereas the other models realise time in ways that must be translated back into an ordering to be read or compiled.
Universality for each of the other models is in turn established by reproducing circuits, so the circuit model is already the common form against which the others are defined.

That the circuit model is the most readily expressed is therefore a fact about the programmer's surface rather than about computational power or physical convenience.
The measurement-based and adiabatic models hold physical advantages in resource preparation and in error suppression that the circuit model lacks~\cite{raussendorf2001oneway, aharonov2007adiabatic}.
Its dominance both mirrors and inverts the classical history that motivates the criteria for a good abstract machine.
Classical circuits were expressively complete yet unusable until an instruction set supplied composable operations matching the way programmers reason~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
Quantum computation has instead adopted the circuit model as its default surface.
A contingent coincidence between one model and the structure of sequential composition thereby hardens into the assumption that time is a gate sequence.
That assumption ascends into intermediate representations and compilers and forces the other models to be implemented as conversions from circuits rather than from their own principles~\cite{aharonov2007adiabatic}.
The models of computation settle what can be computed without settling how a computation should be expressed to the programmer, which is the work of a programming paradigm rather than a computation model.

\subsection{Programming paradigms}\label{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:programming_paradigms}

Section~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:models_of_computation} compared the models of computation by what each takes a computation to be; the paradigms surveyed here differ instead in how they relate control flow to quantum data.
That relationship fixes whether control stays a classical scaffold issuing operations from outside the quantum state or is itself drawn into superposition alongside the data it governs.
The circuit model remains the common substrate beneath either choice, so the difference lies not in what is ultimately run but in how that circuit is built, controlled and executed on the underlying hardware.
Where control sits governs what a language can express, how much of the quantum machinery the programmer manages by hand and how the cost of a program must be reasoned about.
Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:quantum_programming:control_paradigms} illustrates how these parameters are exhibited in both quantum and classical control paradigms.
The remainder of the section examines the paradigms in the context of control, weighing the expressiveness of each against their physical and programmatic tradeoffs.

The dominant paradigm confines superposition to the data and keeps control flow classical, a division the foundational semantics for this paradigm capture in the phrase \textit{quantum data, classical control}~\cite{selinger2004towards}.
The restriction falls on control flow, not on operations: a qubit in superposition may still act as the control of a gate such as \texttt{CNOT}, which applies a fixed unitary unconditionally, but no program turns the choice of which statement executes next into a quantum value~\cite{selinger2004towards}.
The hardware picture beneath it, the quantum random access machine of Section~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:models_of_computation}, pairs a classical controller with a quantum co-processor that it prepares, transforms and measures, returning only classical outcomes to the controlling program~\cite{knill1996pseudocode, wang2023qrasp}.
Its hold comes from inheriting classical reasoning intact: established control constructs, type systems and program-structuring intuitions carry over unchanged and the canonical algorithms, Shor, Grover and the quantum Fourier transform, already take the form of classical orchestration around quantum subroutines~\cite{selinger2004towards}.
Within this paradigm languages still differ in programming style, functional or imperative, a difference easily mistaken for what lets a language enforce no-cloning statically rather than checking it at run time.
What determines this is whether the type system treats qubits as linear resources: where a qubit can be aliased under more than one name a compiler cannot decide statically that two names denote distinct qubits, so no-cloning falls to run-time checks, whereas a linear discipline that forbids duplication settles distinctness in the types~\cite{selinger2004towards}.
Programming style is therefore incidental to the guarantee, which follows from resource discipline and not from functional or imperative form.
An imperative, high-level design reaches the same static guarantees: \textbf{Silq} enforces them through a linear discipline for consumable values and a non-linear one for constants, marking classically describable functions \texttt{qfree} and read-only inputs \texttt{const}~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
On the same discipline Silq uncomputes temporary values automatically, removing a class of manual ancilla bookkeeping the paradigm would otherwise leave to the programmer~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
The limit on what this paradigm can express follows from the same division.

A program cannot branch on a quantum value while it stays in superposition, because reading that value to settle the branch measures it and collapses the state the branch was meant to depend on~\cite{selinger2004towards}.
A mid-circuit measurement yields a classical bit and later quantum operations may be conditioned on that bit by classical feed-forward, an arrangement standardised in OpenQASM 3 and run on hardware as a dynamic circuit~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
The same mechanism underlies the measurement-based model of Section~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:models_of_computation}, where measuring qubits and feeding the outcomes forward selects the operations that follow.
Control in this paradigm therefore reacts to quantum data once that data is made classical and the round-trip from quantum register to classical decision and back carries its own cost in latency and measured-state fidelity~\cite{dynamicbench2026}.
The programmer still prepares states and applies gates directly and absent a discipline such as Silq's must clear temporary values by hand~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.

The opposing paradigm draws control flow itself into superposition, letting a program branch on quantum data without measuring it first.
The quantum control machine (QCM) surveyed in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:models_of_computation} establishes the structural constraints any such language must satisfy: control constructs must be injective, branch histories must be kept distinguishable and brought back into step before measurement and iteration must be bounded by a classical count~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
The question for language design is how these constraints surface for the programmer.
Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:quantum_programming:reversible_vs_classical_control} provides a comparison of how control functions in quantum and classical settings.

\textbf{Qunity} pursues this by unifying classical and quantum control within one language~\cite{mints2025qunity}.
It answers these requirements with a family of control constructs, \texttt{ctrl}, \texttt{match} and \texttt{pmatch}, that differ in what they demand of their branches and whether they stay reversible~\cite{mints2025qunity}.
\texttt{pmatch} accepts only cases that are orthogonal and pure, that is, perfectly distinguishable from one another and free of residual entanglement, which lets it discard the branch not taken automatically~\cite{mints2025qunity}.
\texttt{ctrl} permits cases that may leave intermediate values behind, so the programmer must state how those values are erased~\cite{mints2025qunity}.
\texttt{match} treats its cases as classical and gives up reversibility~\cite{mints2025qunity}.
Selecting among these constructs requires the programmer to reason about whether the relevant expressions are orthogonal and pure, which are properties of the underlying quantum representation rather than of the intended program logic~\cite{mints2025qunity}.
The language compiles to OpenQASM 3 and runs on hardware, so its constructs are realisable; what they do not remove is the programmer's need to reason at the level of qubits and circuits to apply them correctly~\cite{mints2025qunity}.
A second design, \textbf{Lambda-SX}, enlarges what can serve as classical control data by recording in its type system the measurement basis in which a value is known~\cite{diazcaro2025lambdasx}.
A value guaranteed to lie in a known basis gives a definite outcome when measured in that basis, so it can be copied and used to drive branching as though it were classical, without breaching no-cloning~\cite{diazcaro2025lambdasx}.
The system is an early proof of concept, restricted to first-order functions, confined to single-qubit bases and silent on entanglement~\cite{diazcaro2025lambdasx}.
The QCM analysis also bears on how the cost of a program must be reasoned about, not only on what the program can express~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
A loop must run for a number of iterations fixed in advance to stay reversible, since a bound that depended on a quantum value would have no definite count until measured~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Where the natural bound is a quantum value, the loop must therefore commit to a fixed classical count, padding the shorter cases with idle steps so that every branch finishes together~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
A conditional carries a related cost, since the hardware evaluates every branch rather than only the one selected, so the running time of a branch adds to the others rather than replacing them~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Both designs move control into the quantum domain, yet both still work qubit by qubit~\cite{mints2025qunity, diazcaro2025lambdasx}.
Each asks the programmer to manage representational detail, orthogonality and purity in Qunity and basis structure in Lambda-SX, so the expressiveness this paradigm adds comes at the cost of abstracting quantum-specific constraints~\cite{mints2025qunity, diazcaro2025lambdasx}.

A more recent design combines classical and quantum control within a single calculus, rather than committing a program to one paradigm or the other~\cite{dave2025combining}.
It does so through a modality that embeds the reversible, pure quantum-control fragment, in which branching stays in superposition, within a larger mixed-state setting where measurement has occurred and control proceeds on classical outcomes~\cite{dave2025combining}.
Measurement provides the only irreversible operation and joins the two fragments~\cite{dave2025combining}.
A program transforms entangled state under quantum control; the classical outcome of a measurement then drives ordinary branching~\cite{dave2025combining}.
Programs that interleave the two, such as teleportation with its measurement-dependent corrections, are expressible within a single language as a result~\cite{dave2025combining}.
The boundary between the paradigms is made explicit in the type system, so the programmer takes on that boundary in exchange for static guarantees that each fragment is used in keeping with its discipline~\cite{dave2025combining}.
The system is total by construction and admits no general recursion; it is presented as a semantics rather than an implemented language with a compilation path~\cite{dave2025combining}.
Reconciling the two paradigms within one calculus provides an avenue to avoid choosing between them and demonstrates formally the required guarantees to do so.
Like the quantum-control languages above, however, the design remains a research artefact rather than a system in routine use; in routine use, quantum programming keeps control flow classical.

A survey of usage finds Qiskit, Cirq and Q\# the most widely used systems, each expressing quantum computation as circuits directed from a classical program rather than drawing control into superposition~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
The designs that branch on quantum data remain research prototypes by comparison, demonstrated rather than adopted~\cite{mints2025qunity, diazcaro2025lambdasx}.
They are more expressive in principle, admitting decisions that depend on a quantum state without measuring it first, but they still require the programmer to reason about the underlying representation and carry the execution costs the QCM analysis sets out~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Classical control forgoes those decisions, since a branch on a quantum value must measure it and collapse the superposition and takes in exchange the established reasoning of classical programming~\cite{selinger2004towards}.
Practice therefore keeps to a classical program directing quantum subroutines, the arrangement that demands the least new reasoning of the programmer, while quantum control stays confined to languages not yet in general use~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.

\subsection{Model-invariant constraints}\label{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:model_invariant_constraints}
Subsections~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:models_of_computation}and~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:programming_paradigms} show that models and paradigms differ in what they take a program to be but not the constraints a programming system must satisfy.
State evolution in any realisable quantum system must be reversible and norm-preserving rather than many-to-one~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Under unitarity, several moves that are benign in classical programming become active semantic obligations: discarding a temporary value, merging control-flow histories, and treating a read as a passive observation each assumes that information can be forgotten and each fails when that information is quantum.

Uncomputation is the clearest instance of a model-invariant constraint on abstraction.
A temporary register cannot in general be treated as a disposable state, since it can remain entangled with the data that is later measured.
If a program discards such a register without uncomputing it, the discarded subsystem remains correlated with the retained subsystem.
The joint state is no longer separable as a tensor product of an output state and a fixed ancilla state.
Uncomputation is therefore not an implementation detail of a compiler backend.
It is a semantic obligation that a programming system must discharge either by requiring explicit clean-up constructs or by enforcing conditions under which intermediate state can be dropped safely~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.

A second recurring constraint is injectivity of state transitions.
Classical control flow relies on many-to-one updates, since a conditional jump overwrites the program counter and merges multiple execution histories into a single continuation.
When the program counter is lifted into superposition, a non-injective control transition cannot be realised as a unitary evolution~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Retaining full history information restores injectivity by construction, but the formal analysis shows that the retained history can become entangled with the data.
The joint state cannot be rewritten as a tensor product in which the history can be discarded before measurement~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Injectivity therefore functions as a constraint on which control abstractions are even definable, not merely on how efficiently they can be implemented~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.

The same requirement for representation to be independent of construction history reappears in quantum data structures.
If the same logical value can occupy multiple physical configurations that depend on the sequence of updates, then computations that create the value by different routes need not interfere predictably~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.

Control flow imposes an additional constraint on time.
If the number of loop iterations depends on a quantum value, then the control record becomes branch-dependent and remains correlated with the data unless the execution is forced to rejoin at a common program point~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Synchronisation provides this rejoining condition and explains why quantum control models restrict iteration to classically bounded loops, typically realised by padding shorter branches to a fixed bound~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.

The same pattern, in which a classical abstraction assumes information can be forgotten, recurs when a quantum program accesses memory.
The classical model treats a read as a passive operation of constant cost that leaves the storage unchanged.
In a coherent quantum access model a read is implemented as a unitary controlled operation that copies the value into an output register, creating correlations between the address, the output and the access mechanism~\cite{jaques2025qram}.
Read and write are therefore not cleanly separable primitives; the access leaves behind correlations that must be accounted for, so the classical assumption of passivity does not hold.
The assumption of constant cost fails as well.
If the address may be in superposition, the mechanism must behave consistently across all addresses without revealing which address was used; otherwise information about the address leaks and coherence is lost~\cite{jaques2025qram}.
Scaling coherent access forces either per-query work that grows with the size of the memory, or hardware whose component error must shrink as the memory grows~\cite{jaques2025qram}.
Coherent memory access has the same structural problem as control flow: a classical abstraction that treats information erasure as cost-free becomes semantically active when the data is quantum.

The control-flow results supply a worked case of how physical constraints become programmer-facing obligations.
Classical control abstractions implicitly rely on history erasure, since they merge multiple execution paths into a single continuation by overwriting the program counter~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
When control itself is placed into superposition, this erasure is no longer an internal implementation detail that can be corrected by a compiler.
It becomes a semantic property of the abstraction, since any control construct that merges histories without retaining recoverable information has a non-injective transition semantics and therefore cannot be realised by unitary evolution~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.

Injectivity is therefore not merely a theorem about conditional jumps.
It is a design rule for control abstractions: the abstraction must preserve enough information that the control state can be reversed and any information it introduces must remain recoverable until it has been safely cleared~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
A Landauer-style embedding restores injectivity by retaining history, but it also illustrates the second obligation that injectivity alone does not settle~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
The retained history is part of the quantum state and it can become entangled with the data state.
A language, therefore, cannot offer control constructs that generate arbitrary control history and then promise to remove it later.
The abstraction must be structured so that the control record is guaranteed to become disposable at a well-defined point~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.

Synchronisation is the condition that makes this disposability possible~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
A synchronised construct ensures that every branch of a superposed execution reaches the same program point at the same step, so that the control registers can be cleared without leaving them correlated with the data~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Iteration cannot be expressed as an unbounded while-loop whose bound depends on a quantum value, since such a loop would halt at branch-dependent times and leave a branch-dependent control record~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Quantum control models therefore require a classical bound on iteration and must ensure that all branches execute to that bound, either by padding with no-ops or by an equivalent synchronising discipline~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
These conditions introduce a cognitive obligation that classical structured programming does not impose.
The programmer must reason not only about which computation is performed, but about what control information is preserved, when it becomes separable from the data and how it can be cleared without changing observable behaviour.

Silq, Tower and Qunity respond to the same obligation by relocating programmer obligations, rather than eliminating them.
Each system enforces a discipline that makes uncomputation and clean resource disposal possible, but they place the proof burden in different parts of the programming model.

Silq makes uncomputation an ambient guarantee by restricting when intermediate values may be discarded and by distinguishing classically describable values from superpositions~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
The programmer must maintain an explicit mental partition between values that behave like classical data and values that may be in superposition, since only the former can be freely copied, stored and used to justify later clean-up~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
The same obligation appears in function design.
A programmer must anticipate whether an input will be treated as read-only or consumed and must choose annotations that make the information needed for safe uncomputation available when the temporary value is released~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
This is a cognitive burden because it requires reasoning about future reversibility at the point of writing ordinary-looking expressions, rather than at an explicit clean-up boundary.

Tower takes the opposite approach and exposes the clean-up actions directly as part of the operational story of the language.
Reversibility is enforced by explicitly distinguishing assignment from unassignment and by using swaps to control where information resides~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Intermediate artefacts must be returned to a known form by explicit programmer action, rather than being assumed reclaimable by the language or compiler~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
This makes correctness sensitive to bookkeeping errors, such as needing to track where a value currently points to unassign it after pointer mutation.

Qunity places the burden on the structure of control itself by providing multiple control operators with distinct side conditions, including an explicitly orthogonality-constrained pattern matching form~\cite{mints2025qunity}.
A programmer must select between control forms whose preconditions differ, rather than relying on a single uniform branching construct~\cite{mints2025qunity}.
This shifts the work from writing explicit clean-up code towards ensuring that a chosen control abstraction is admissible for the values being branched over.
It also makes control-flow expressiveness depend on semantic properties of states, rather than only on program structure.

The constraints in this subsection are not independent quirks of quantum mechanics.
They arise when a classical abstraction assumes that information can be forgotten.
Unitarity turns that assumption into an explicit obligation.

Each route improves correctness and can make certain costs legible.
However, measured against the working definition of abstraction in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction}, they do not yet constitute an ideal conceptual tier for programmers.
They relocate information-flow obligations, but they also keep those obligations inside the programmer’s working set as pervasive typing decisions, explicit reversals or semantic side conditions.
The remaining burden therefore appears structural rather than incidental and the design task becomes to concentrate it into a small number of stable, local concepts rather than distributing it across ordinary programming decisions.

\subsection{The structure of the design space}\label{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:design_space}
Quantum programming systems differ not in whether they satisfy the model-invariant constraints established in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:quantum_programming:model_invariant_constraints} but in where those constraints surface and who must reason about them.
Four dimensions of variation capture how systems distribute responsibility for those constraints.
Control paradigm fixes whether control remains classical or enters superposition.
Representation determines which execution model is the default surface and how directly it exposes circuits and qubits.
Obligation discipline decides whether reversibility and disposal are enforced statically, left to explicit constructs or made contingent on semantic side conditions.
Quantum bleed-through measures how strongly quantum-mechanical constraints intrude into ordinary programming decisions.

The dimensions above separate into constraints forced by physical realisability and choices that relocate those constraints.
The forced constraints are those that follow from reversible, norm-preserving evolution and therefore survive any change of control paradigm or representation.
Intermediate state cannot be treated as disposable unless it is made separable from the output, so uncomputation functions as a semantic obligation rather than a backend optimisation.
Control abstractions cannot merge histories irreversibly when control is placed into superposition, since non-injective transition semantics do not lift to unitary evolution without retaining recoverable history.
Retaining history information restores reversibility, but it can also entangle control records with data, so the control trace must be structured so it becomes removable at a well-defined point.
Synchronisation provides that condition by requiring that superposed branches rejoin at the same program point at the same step so that the control state can be separated from the data state before termination.
This constrains iteration and makes branch-dependent halting structurally unavailable.

The chosen dimensions determine where these obligations appear and who is required to reason about them.
A language may keep control classical and accept measurement as the boundary at which branching is permitted, or it may admit quantum control and instead require reversibility-preserving, synchronised control constructs.
These positions are not confined to separate languages.
A single calculus can embed reversible quantum control into a measured, mixed-state environment, so that classical and quantum control coexist under one type discipline rather than fixing a program to either.
Control in this sense forms a conscious design choice rather than being forced.

A language may adopt the circuit model as an explicit surface, or it may abstract over it while still depending on circuits as the default translation form used to define and exchange computations.
A language may concentrate obligation discipline in static guarantees, shifting proof burden into types and annotations, or it may expose explicit clean-up actions, or it may make familiar constructs contingent on semantic side conditions.
A language may reduce quantum bleed-through by discharging representational obligations below the surface, but the overall system must still ensure that information is preserved, separated or removed in a way that does not change observable behaviour.

The first groups control paradigm with representation, since these choices jointly determine how computation is expressed and driven.
The second groups obligation discipline with quantum bleed-through, since these choices determine where the model-invariant constraints are discharged and how directly they intrude into ordinary programming decisions.

Control and representation correlate, since models that encode computation as an operator sequence naturally align with classical orchestration.
Models that encode computation into a resource state, a Hamiltonian or a fixed update rule relocate what functions as the primary program object.
Obligation discipline and quantum bleed-through also correlate, since obligations discharged through explicit constructs or semantic side conditions typically require the programmer to track representational detail more directly than obligations enforced below the surface.

The map places the models and paradigms where their commitments force programmer-facing structure.
The absence of mature language-level treatments for non-circuit execution models leaves their rows comparatively under-specified in programmer-facing terms, even where the underlying models are well-developed.
That absence is consistent with circuit dominance, since models whose programs are graphs, Hamiltonians or lattice configurations are typically presented as compilation targets rather than as primary programming surfaces.

Some dimensions in Tables~\ref{tab:lit_rev:quantum_programming:design_space_map_ctrl_rep} and~\ref{tab:lit_rev:quantum_programming:design_space_map_obl_bleed} have clear classical analogues.
Control paradigm resembles the classical separation between pure computation and its orchestration, even where modern systems blur that separation through effects and interaction.
Obligation discipline also has classical precedents, since type systems and ownership disciplines relocate memory safety and resource management obligations between the programmer and the compiler.
Representation also has classical analogues, since functional and imperative paradigms privilege different primary descriptions of computation, expression evaluation versus state update, even when they compile to similar targets.
The analogy is limited, since these paradigms typically share a common execution substrate and differ mainly in surface structure and reasoning principles.
Quantum execution models differ at the level of what constitutes a well-formed program, so representation choices couple more directly to execution structure.
The measurement-based model makes this coupling explicit by shifting the program from an operator sequence to a resource state and a measurement pattern, so the representational choice changes which objects function as the basic units of composition.

The remaining structure is specific to quantum computation.
The forced constraints arise from the need to preserve information under reversible evolution, so ordinary abstraction moves that rely on discarding state or merging histories acquire semantic consequences.
Relocating these constraints below the surface can reduce quantum bleed-through, but it does so by shifting obligations into compilation invariants, runtime protocols or verification conditions rather than by eliminating them.

The language-family survey in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:qpl} evaluates each system against these four dimensions.
Execution model determines the computational form a system assumes and how transparent its lowering is to intermediate representations and hardware targets.
Compositional opacity measures whether combining program fragments leaks quantum state at module boundaries.
Together with control paradigm, representation, obligation discipline and quantum bleed-through, these criteria supply an evaluative lens for assessing each presented system.
