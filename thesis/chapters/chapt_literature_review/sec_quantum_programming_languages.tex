\section{Quantum Programming Languages}\label{sec:lit_rev:qpl}
Programming languages are the tool with which computer scientists and software engineers convey their algorithmic intent to machines for execution.
As described in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction} programming languages come in many types and families, each serving a different role within the execution stack or compilation chain.
These roles include high-level languages, designed to make human intent as easy as possible to express, intermediate representations which make program structure explicit for compiler analysis and transformation and assembly languages which describe execution in terms machines directly operate on.

This section explores the current state of quantum programming languages; those intended to execute on quantum computers.
It examines which layers of the compilation chain are represented, what they offer and whether they support the expression of human intent without requiring programmers to reason at the level of the quantum machine.
The evidence shows genuine progress across all layers; no existing system, however, enables sustained human-centred reasoning: the programmer's required mental model remains machine-centred throughout.

\subsection{Scope and Vocabulary}\label{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:scope}
The review presented in this section will analyse quantum programming languages across four categories or families.
Each of those families is described in Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:qpl:language_families}.
The definitions of these families, their taxonomy and critical analyses are expanded in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:taxonomy}.
Other frameworks assess quantum languages on their semantic or syntactic principles, such as imperative and functional languages~\cite{Gay2006QPLSurvey}.
The review presented here does not do that and instead focuses on the intended purpose in the compilation stack.

Each family represents a different role in the compilation and execution of quantum programs or provides ergonomic or abstraction benefits.
The families of languages selected represent languages that are used by programmers in the development of quantum programs or those required to demonstrate the full compilation chains and comparisons of complexity.
From those criteria languages eligible for inclusion are embedded frameworks in classical languages, intermediate representation languages, quantum assembly languages and high-level quantum development languages.
Languages that solely represent hardware control and vendor or specific machine level languages are excluded from review as they are either not intended for human development or fail to add value to discussions about abstraction.

To evaluate the current state of quantum programming languages, the review will consider the following elements of each language and family:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Abstraction level: The degree to which a system exposes machine-level quantum detail to a programmer~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy}.
    \item Required quantum knowledge: What quantum mechanical understanding a programmer must have to productively use the language~\cite{Myers2016PLUsabilitySIG, ferreira2024qplusage}.
    \item System portability: Whether the language can be used to target multiple hardware backends without programmer intervention~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy, nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
\end{itemize}
Together these criteria represent existing evaluation frameworks and known areas of friction for programmers of quantum programming.
The level to which a language adheres to them is a signifier of its ability to advance the current state of quantum programming languages.

Each language family will have a different aspirational level for the criteria presented, intermediate representations will require a higher degree of quantum knowledge than high-level languages.
The criteria are applied contextually based on the purpose of each language family.

\subsection{Taxonomy and the Missing Tier}\label{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:taxonomy}
Sections~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:levels_elements} and~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:looking_forward} established that quantum programming lacks a machine-independent source layer.
This section grounds that finding in an examination of what each language family aspires to and whether any family achieves it.

The defining aspiration of quantum programming language development is quantum-agnostic application development~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
A family of languages that fulfills this aspiration would not require fundamental knowledge of quantum mechanics or quantum circuits to program.
Variations of this aspiration exist; some argue that quantum agnosticism could be achieved by improving circuit generation techniques to remove quantum pre-requisite knowledge~\cite{cobb2022higherlevelabstractions}.
Others identify a lack of separation between instructions and circuit primitives as preventing the formulation of a genuine source-layer language~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
Additional analysis has shown a lack of complex data structures and quantum control sequence support~\cite{Gay2006QPLSurvey} as further barriers.

Regardless of the path, the literature converges on fundamental principles such a language family would adhere to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Minimal or no understanding of fundamental quantum mechanics required to write programs.
    \item No direct manipulation of the quantum circuit model or equivalents in other hardware.
    \item The programmers mental model exists in the problem space; not quantum mechanics or circuits.
\end{itemize}

Four language families are identified in the literature and introduced in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:scope}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item High-level
    \item Embedded framework
    \item Intermediate representation
    \item Assembly
\end{itemize}

Each offers different aspirational goals in the quantum programming domain.
A summary is provided in Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:qpl:language_family_taxonomy}.
This subsection characterises each family; Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:reading} examines them in detail.

Assembly languages such as OpenQASM aspire to offer a portable, hardware-agnostic specification of quantum circuits.
They describe circuit primitives alongside hardware concerns such as pulses, timing delays and qubit rotations or gates~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
OpenQASM achieves these aspirations.
Quantum assembly languages differ fundamentally from their classical analogues; they don't describe microprocessor instructions, they describe circuits.
Quantum assembly languages at their most fundamental are a mathematical abstraction over circuit construction, offering hardware independent ways to describe that structure.
While they do offer lower level control such as pulses and timing, predominantly \textit{how} to physically realise a logical gate is left up to toolchains specific to the hardware.
Classical assembly languages such as RISC or x86 conversely describe instructions given to hardware but have no mathematical description of how those instructions are physically built in a circuit.

Intermediate representations (IRs) offer the same aspirations for quantum programming as their classical counterparts.
A productive IR is machine-independent and stable across the compilation stack; both forward and backward compatible~\cite{irreview2024}.
IRs retain the intent of the source layer while enabling compiler optimisation passes and formal verification~\cite{irreview2024}.
Current quantum IRs may or may not encode quantum-physical constraints such as no-cloning or teleportation.
QIR and Q-MLIR achieve these outcomes~\cite{irreview2024}.

Embedded frameworks enable ecosystem integration for remote execution and simulation of quantum programs from within a classical programming language, providing ergonomic tools for common quantum algorithms and circuit compilation for quantum execution systems.
Qiskit~\cite{aleksandrowicz2019qiskit} is a representative example, offering Python libraries for circuit construction, compilation across multiple hardware backends and remote execution.
PennyLane~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane} offers comparable tooling with a focus on variational quantum circuits and machine learning techniques.

High-level languages aspire to provide a layer where algorithmic thinking drives the programmer.
These languages aim to provide an abstraction level where the compiler can reason about quantum obligations such as physical correctness and resource management.
As Section~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:in_quantum_computing} showed, Silq, Qmod and Q\# offer many high-level tools to support algorithmic programming.
However, many of these tools still require the programmer to understand the quantum-mechanical and circuit-level principles underlying their programs.
High-level languages do not divorce the quantum from quantum programming.

The taxonomy reveals a missing ``fifth-tier'' of quantum programming languages.
The quantum-agnostic, application programming paradigm aspired to in the literature is not realisable with any of the language families presented.
Assembly and intermediate representations are explicitly quantum by design, requiring a fundamental understanding of circuit primitives and hardware concerns.
Embedded frameworks were not intended to produce quantum-agnosticism; their goal is to improve access to near-term quantum computers and remote execution of quantum circuit definitions.
Embedded frameworks achieve this effectively, but it is not an adequate solution to the missing tier.
High-level languages come closest, but their fundamental coupling to quantum circuit primitives and resource management requires the programmer to have a quantum mechanical mental model.
These languages do not enable problems and solutions to be formulated purely algorithmically.

\subsection{Reading the Language Landscape}\label{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:reading}
Quantum programming language development continues to focus on improving computability and tractability within quantum domains rather than focusing on improving the experience of programmers~\cite{corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness}.
Assessing programming languages solely against their computability biases success towards machine concerns rather than human factors and programmer experience.

Current quantum programming languages suffer from a usability issue failing to be addressed at a fundamental level~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage, nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
A survey of active quantum programming projects on GitHub showed a lack of novel development or algorithmic investigation~\cite{murillo2022sequantumprogramming}.
Surveys of embedded framework usage found that their popularity can be attributed to the improved programmer experience when using a classical language; however, programmers still struggled with the circuit architecture~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
Programmers consistently report quantum domain knowledge, vocabulary and concepts as barriers to entry and understanding of quantum languages~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage, murillo2022sequantumprogramming}.

High-level programming languages realise the abstraction requirements described in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction}.
In classical programming language theory they provide a control plane that reduces a programmer's exposure to machine concerns~\cite{scott2009plp}.

In the remainder of this subsection, the various language families in quantum computing are described in detail.
Their definitions are presented and members of the family analysed for their features and success as programming languages.
Languages are assessed on the degree to which they support computational thinking paradigms~\cite{Wing2006ComputationalThinking}, graded based on their family and position in the compilation stack.

Each language family is assessed on its suitability to support the ideal quantum abstract machine~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines} described in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction} with respect to its role in compilation.
High-level languages are assessed with additional rigour against their ability to shield programmers from inherent constraints in quantum programming not present in classical programming.
\textit{Representation exposure}; the degree to which a programmer is required to reason about internal quantum state directly, due to the inability to copy or inspect it without disturbing the computation.
\textit{Semantic mismatch}; the degree to which invalid equivalence with classical concepts is assumed~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.

\subsubsection{Assembly Languages}\label{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:assembly}
Assembly languages describe quantum computation at the level of the execution model: explicit instructions over named quantum resources, applied in a sequence that hardware can directly interpret.
OpenQASM is the canonical representative of this family~\cite{cross2017openqasm, cross2022openqasm3}.

OpenQASM states its primary goal is to serve as an intermediate representation for communicating with quantum hardware, with allowances made for human usability~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
The language describes quantum circuits using explicit qubit register declarations, named gate operations and measurement instructions.
Gates are drawn from the CNOT-plus-SU(2) basis: the controlled-NOT gate, which entangles two qubits, and the special unitary group SU(2), which encompasses all single-qubit rotations; together these are enough for universal quantum computation~\cite{cross2017openqasm}.

Version 3.0 extended the language with classical types (\texttt{int}, \texttt{float}, \texttt{bool}, \texttt{angle}), control flow constructs (\texttt{if}/\texttt{while}/\texttt{for}), real-time feed-forward and explicit timing and scheduling directives~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
An optional pulse-level calibration mechanism, introduced via the \texttt{defcal} keyword and OpenPulse grammar, allows gate implementations to be overridden with hardware-specific microwave pulse sequences applied directly to qubits, extending the language's reach below the circuit model when required~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
Like classical assembly languages, which provide a symbolic, near-one-to-one representation of machine instructions~\cite{scott2009plp}, OpenQASM presents quantum operations in terms that correspond directly to what the underlying hardware performs.
Portability is achieved through a shared vocabulary of quantum operations: hardware-specific gate sets and timing constraints are resolved downstream, not at the language level.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:qpl:assembly_comparison} the assembly label, while conventional, overstates the analogy with classical computing.
Classical assembly converges programmer vocabulary with hardware execution: a MOV or ADD instruction corresponds directly to what the processor does.
OpenQASM, by contrast, describes logical gate primitives that are hardware-independent by design; the physical realisation; pulse shaping, basis gate decomposition, timing calibration; lives below it in vendor toolchains the programmer never touches.
The quantum programmer-facing model is therefore removed from the specific hardware, yet the full cognitive burden of circuit-level reasoning is still a hard requirement.

In practice, OpenQASM programs are authored by compilers and toolchains rather than human programmers directly.
Evaluating OpenQASM using the framework established in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:reading} can be achieved by analysing a representative workflow; the analysis surfaces the abstraction level and quantum knowledge requirements the language imposes.

Creating a superposition and conditioning later execution on a measurement outcome is one such workflow.
To achieve that workflow, the programmer must declare a qubit register, apply a Hadamard gate (\texttt{h q}) to create superposition, collect a measurement outcome into a classical bit register and write a control flow conditioned on that result as described in Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:qpl:open_qasm_example}.
At no point does the language offer a layer at which this workflow can be expressed without direct reference to qubits, gates and measurement mechanics: the circuit is the expression of thought.

The classical control flow introduced in version 3.0 merits specific attention because it is presented as evidence of abstraction-level progress~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
The syntax is C-like: typed variables, \texttt{while} loops, \texttt{if}/\texttt{else} constructs and familiar operator precedence.
A programmer arriving from classical software development will recognise these constructs immediately.
What the syntax does not expose is the constraint on their semantics: classical operations in OpenQASM execute on a real-time quantum controller, bounded by the feed-forward window imposed by qubit coherence times; operations that cannot complete within this window must be delegated to \texttt{extern} functions, which are opaque to the OpenQASM layer and executed by an external classical processor~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
The specification acknowledges this directly: the language is not designed for general-purpose classical computation~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
The C-like syntax provides coordination scaffolding for quantum circuits; it does not provide a general classical programming model, and it leaves the quantum substrate entirely unchanged.

The structural position of OpenQASM provides insight into the difficulties being faced with improved abstraction in quantum computing~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
Classical abstraction could progressively absorb hardware detail because the lowest programmer concepts were already close to the machine~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
The quantum stack conceptually begins by conflating mathematical circuit description with low-level programming
Abstracting away from it requires not just hiding hardware detail but replacing a circuit-oriented mental model entirely; a qualitatively greater undertaking.

OpenQASM achieves what it sets out to do: it provides a portable, complete execution-level specification that functions as a hardware-independent circuit interchange format.
In classical programming language theory, however, raising a language's abstraction level means reducing the programmer's required exposure to machine-level concerns~\cite{scott2009plp}; adding features that expand expressiveness without reducing that exposure does not constitute a change of level.
The programmer using OpenQASM, regardless of version, must reason in terms of qubit registers, gate operations and measurement outcomes.
The language's position in the compilation stack is fixed by design intent, not by implementation immaturity.

\subsubsection{Intermediate Representations}\label{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:ir}
Intermediate representations occupy a distinct role in any compilation stack: they are machine-independent substrates designed not for human authorship, but for compiler analysis, optimisation and transformation~\cite{scott2009plp, irreview2024}.
In classical programming language theory, an IR sits between the source layer and assembly; high-level source code is lowered into an IR, optimisation passes are applied and the result is lowered again into assembly for hardware execution~\cite{scott2009plp}.
Two representative quantum IRs are examined here.
Quantum Intermediate Representation (QIR) is an LLVM-based standard developed by the QIR Alliance~\cite{qiralliance_spec}.
Q-MLIR is a family of MLIR-based quantum dialects enabling progressive lowering through multiple compiler stages~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
Both languages claim to offer a common substrate enabling multiple front-end languages, reusable compiler passes and a formal verification surface across the quantum software stack~\cite{irreview2024}.

As with assembly, the IR label implies a classical relationship that does not hold in the quantum stack.
In classical computing, IR sits \textit{above} assembly in the pipeline; it is the layer that assembly is compiled \textit{from}.
In quantum computing the pipeline inverts: OpenQASM programs are parsed into MLIR quantum dialect representations, progressively lowered to LLVM IR adherent to QIR specifications and transformed into backend-specific executable code~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
Quantum IR therefore sits \textit{below} quantum assembly in the compilation pipeline, a position with no direct classical analogue.
In classical stacks there is nothing below assembly except machine code, which is an encoding concern rather than a programmable layer.
This inversion compounds the structural asymmetry identified in Section~\ref{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:assembly}: the quantum programmer-facing floor is already more abstract than classical assembly relative to the hardware, and below it lies a rich IR infrastructure that classical abstraction journeys never encountered.

QIR is constructed as an extension of LLVM IR, the foundational intermediate representation of the classical LLVM compiler infrastructure~\cite{scott2009plp}.
Where LLVM IR represents values as typed references that classical code can read,write and pass freely, QIR introduces two dedicated abstract resource types: one for qubits and one for measurement results~\cite{qiralliance_spec}.
These types are defined without any internal structure accessible to the programmer; classical code cannot read, copy, or directly manipulate them, and all interactions must go through a defined set of quantum operations~\cite{qiralliance_spec}.
This restriction encodes a foundational quantum constraint at the IR layer: qubits are physical resources that cannot be duplicated or inspected without disturbing their state.
All quantum operations in QIR are declared as external functions whose implementations are deferred to hardware backends.
The IR specifies \textit{that} an operation exists and what resources it acts on, but not \textit{how} it is physically realised, allowing the same QIR program to target diverse hardware without modification~\cite{qiralliance_spec}.
An explicit resource lifecycle model tracks when qubits are acquired and released.
Hooks are provided for detecting unsafe patterns such as operating on a qubit after it has been freed, analogous to memory safety analysis in classical compilation~\cite{qiralliance_spec, qirformalization2024}.

A more fundamental limitation is that QIR's specification remains informal, described in natural language without formal semantics~\cite{qirformalization2024}.
Formalisation efforts~\cite{qirformalization2024} are themselves motivated by this gap, noting that the absence of formal definitions creates interpretational ambiguity when implementing compilation toolchains.
A concrete illustration of this instability is a known incompatibility between QIR's typed resource handles and a change introduced in LLVM 15, which replaced all typed pointer representations with a single generic pointer type; this conflict remains unresolved in the QIR specification~\cite{qiralliance_spec}.
Taken together, these findings position QIR as a promising but incomplete substrate whose verification guarantees are real but not yet fully realised.

Q-MLIR approaches the IR problem through a different architectural strategy: rather than a single standardised target format, it provides a framework for building domain-specific compiler pipelines using MLIR's dialect mechanism~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
A dialect defines a set of operations specific to a domain, each with a name, typed inputs and outputs and compile-time attributes; conversion rules specify how operations in one dialect are progressively rewritten into another until a common hardware-compatible base is reached.
A representative quantum dialect provides a minimal operation set: register allocation and deallocation, qubit addressing within a register, and general gate application~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
A key design feature is the use of static single assignment (SSA) form, in which each value is defined exactly once and consumed explicitly.
SSA applied to quantum resources makes dataflow visible to the compiler and allows constraints such as the no cloning theorem to be enforced structurally rather than by convention~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
Q-MLIR and QIR are linked through compilation.
MLIR dialects provide the multi-level lowering infrastructure.
QIR serves as the standardised LLVM-compatible target MLIR lowers into.
MLIR's built-in LLVM dialect provides established conversion routines, so a quantum dialect need only define rewrite rules from its own operations to LLVM primitives and the remaining lowering path is inherited~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
The viability of this approach in production toolchains is demonstrated by Catalyst~\cite{PennyLaneCatalystMLIRDialectsDocs}, PennyLane's MLIR-based compiler.
Catalyst lowers differentiable quantum programs through a custom quantum dialect pipeline to hardware-executable targets, illustrating how the dialect mechanism scales from prototype to industrial deployment.

Comparative assessment places both systems similarly: high ratings for hardware agnosticism and optimisation capability, low ratings for tooling accessibility and ease of use~\cite{irreview2024}.
A shared structural limitation reinforces this picture: neither system can distinguish qubits on the same processor from those on separate networked nodes.
Distributed quantum computation is unrepresentable at the IR layer~\cite{irreview2024}.
Proposed extensions~\cite{cardama2024netqir} address this by introducing communication primitives that abstract internode state transfer, mirroring how classical distributed IRs such as OpenCL approach heterogeneous compute.
This suggests that compatibility with classical IR infrastructure is a more productive path than independent reinvention.

The IR family sits below quantum assembly and above hardware execution in the compilation stack, and its intended audience is compiler and toolchain authors rather than application programmers.
Applying the computational thinking framework established in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction}, the IR layer does not support problem-space mental models.
The contribution of IRs is enabling stable, reusable transformations between layers, not reducing the programmer's required quantum vocabulary.
The IR layer is providing the abstraction boundary its design intends: a stable, hardware-agnostic substrate that absorbs low-level concerns below the programmer-facing stack~\cite{irreview2024, qiralliance_spec}.
Whether the layers above it consistently exploit that boundary is explored in discussions on Embedded Frameworks and High level languages.

\subsubsection{Embedded Frameworks}\label{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:frameworks}
Embedded frameworks represent a third design response to the quantum programming problem.
Rather than defining a new standalone language, they extend an existing general-purpose host to accommodate quantum constructs through library APIs and domain-specific abstractions.
Python is the dominant host language in this family, though not the exclusive one.
Haskell-hosted frameworks such as quAPL occupy the same structural position~\cite{corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness}.
The defining design choice reflects a deliberate premise: programmers already possess proficiency in classical languages, and quantum programming tools should meet them there rather than require adoption of a new language environment.
The family's stated goal is not the elimination of quantum prerequisites; it is the acceleration of programmers in expressing quantum algorithms reliably and portably, with a reduction in manually specifying low-level circuit concerns at every step~\cite{corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness}.

Three systems are examined here, selected to represent the breadth and internal limits of this family's design space.
\textbf{Qiskit} is the most widely adopted embedded framework~\cite{murillo2022sequantumprogramming}.
Its model centres on the circuit object as the primary artefact.
The programmer constructs quantum circuits through Python API calls, applies transpilation passes to target a specific backend and submits the compiled circuit for execution or simulation~\cite{aleksandrowicz2019qiskit}.
Hardware portability across backends and access to a mature ecosystem of algorithm libraries are Qiskit's primary value proposition~\cite{aleksandrowicz2019qiskit}.
\textbf{PennyLane} occupies a distinct design position within the family~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane}.
It treats quantum circuits as differentiable functions, \textit{QNodes}, that can be composed with classical computation and differentiated automatically, targeting hybrid quantum-classical machine learning workflows directly.
Its strongest abstraction claim is specific to this workflow class: the variational and optimisation loop is largely managed by the framework, reducing manual programmer burden for that pattern while leaving the general quantum programming model unchanged~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane}.
\textbf{Qrisp} represents the furthest step towards higher abstraction in this family~\cite{seidel2024qrisp}.
Qrisp introduces a typed quantum variable hierarchy, \texttt{QuantumVariable}, \texttt{QuantumFloat}, \texttt{QuantumArray} and \texttt{QuantumDictionary}, alongside dynamic qubit allocation, compiler-managed arithmetic between quantum types and an \texttt{auto_uncompute} decorator.
The decorator automates clean-up of temporary quantum states within a function's scope.
Its design targets top-down algorithm expression, positioning quantum type management as a compiler obligation rather than a programmer task~\cite{seidel2024qrisp}.
In doing so, Qrisp shares design features with standalone high-level languages; the significance of that proximity is examined in the analysis that follows and in Section~\ref{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:highlevel}.

In documented practice, the Qiskit and PennyLane workflows centre on the circuit object or QNode as the primary artefact: the programmer constructs, transforms and executes circuits in Python.
A survey of active quantum programmers found that 83\% come from classical software development backgrounds, confirming that the Python host addresses a documented accessibility gap~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
Repository mining of projects using major quantum frameworks found that most projects replicate known circuits rather than investigate novel algorithms, pointing to a limit on what improved tooling alone unlocks~\cite{murillo2022sequantumprogramming}.
Quantum vocabulary remains the primary reported barrier in both studies: qubits, gates, measurement operations, transpilation constraints and device connectivity limits surface in typical workflows regardless of the ergonomic improvements the framework provides~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage, murillo2022sequantumprogramming}.

PennyLane's differentiable programming model introduces a design that warrants separate examination.
Treating a quantum circuit as a differentiable function allows the variational and optimisation loop to be managed by the framework.
The programmer composes quantum and classical components using automatic differentiation idioms without specifying parameter update mechanics manually~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane}.
Within that workflow class, a class of manual circuit management is removed from the programmer's scope.
Outside it, the general quantum programming model is unchanged; a programmer not working within the variational paradigm faces the same circuit construction requirements as in Qiskit~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane, rodriguezdiaz2024qiskitpennylane}.

Qrisp takes the furthest step within the family toward removing mandatory circuit description from the programmer's workflow.
Its typed quantum variable hierarchy abstracts register allocation, compiler-managed arithmetic and type-level operations, removing the need to specify individual gate sequences for operations such as addition or comparison~\cite{seidel2024qrisp}.
The compiler manages qubit allocation dynamically; the programmer does not sequence or assign registers~\cite{seidel2024qrisp}.
The \texttt{auto_uncompute} decorator addresses the clean-up of temporary quantum state after a function completes, a class of obligation that high-level quantum languages have sought to automate~\cite{bichsel2020silq, seidel2024qrisp}.
The mechanism can be compared to garbage collection in managed languages because both address resource clean-up without explicit action at every call site; the comparison, however, overstates the similarity.
Garbage collection operates transparently and requires the programmer to have no awareness of memory management as a concern.
\texttt{auto_uncompute} is an explicit annotation that must be applied by a programmer who already understands that uncomputation is an obligation.
Automatic uncomputation here is closer to structured resource management patterns such as RAII in C++ or \texttt{defer} in Go, reducing the burden of fulfilling an obligation without removing the programmer's awareness of it~\cite{seidel2024qrisp}.

The limits of Qrisp's abstractions are visible at the points where the type hierarchy cannot generalise further.
Creating superposition within a \texttt{QuantumFloat} requires the programmer to apply a Hadamard gate to a specific qubit index within the composite type.
The framework provides no higher-level construct for initialising a typed variable into superposition, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:qpl:superposition_comparison}~\cite{seidel2024qrisp}.
Instantiating any \texttt{QuantumVariable} requires specifying a qubit count~\cite{seidel2024qrisp}.
Specifying precision for a numerical type is a familiar practice in classical languages: \texttt{int32_t} and \texttt{float64} both encode a storage decision.
In Qrisp, the qubit count carries additional constraints arising from the structure of quantum state: the number of qubits determines representational capacity within the computation, and the programmer must reason about those constraints to write correct programs~\cite{seidel2024qrisp}.
Implementing controlled variants of user-defined operations requires explicit annotation of function definitions and an understanding of the circuit-level distinction between controlled and uncontrolled execution~\cite{seidel2024qrisp}.
Comparative complexity analysis found that Qrisp's array abstractions reduce code verbosity through broadcast operations across registers.
The reduction is in expression length rather than in the quantum knowledge required for correct authorship~\cite{corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness}.

Evaluating the family against the three criteria established in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:scope} clarifies what has and has not been achieved.
Abstraction level has been raised in specific dimensions: resource allocation, arithmetic synthesis and structured uncomputation are managed at a layer above the circuit model~\cite{seidel2024qrisp, aleksandrowicz2019qiskit}.
System portability is a documented contribution across all three systems, with hardware-agnostic compilation pipelines that target multiple backends without programmer intervention~\cite{aleksandrowicz2019qiskit, bergholm2018pennylane}.
Required quantum knowledge is where the family's characteristic limit is most clearly visible.
The embedded framework premise is that programmers can remain within a familiar classical reasoning mode and delegate quantum concerns to the framework~\cite{Wing2006ComputationalThinking}.
The Python layer supports this, but engaging the quantum substrate requires a mode switch into circuit-level reasoning.
Gate selection, qubit wiring, measurement mechanics and transpilation constraints become unavoidable in the typical workflow regardless of which framework is used~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage, murillo2022sequantumprogramming}.
The computational thinking boundary sits inside the typical workflow rather than at the system's edge.

Tooling accessibility and conceptual simplicity are separable dimensions; the embedded framework family has advanced the first without the second~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage, murillo2022sequantumprogramming}.
The literature's stated abstraction trajectory, programming without quantum-mechanical prerequisites~\cite{cobb2022higherlevelabstractions}, is not approached by any system in this family.

Qrisp's position at the family's boundary raises a question for the next family.
Its typed variable hierarchy, compiler-managed uncomputation and dynamic resource allocation are features present in standalone high-level languages such as Silq.
The distinction is the Python host model and the persistence of circuit-level reasoning at specific points in the workflow.
Whether a standalone compiled language with stronger type-theoretic guarantees escapes these same constraints is examined in Section~\ref{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:highlevel}.

\subsubsection{High-Level Languages}\label{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:highlevel}
The languages examined in this section share a common design orientation: they abstract over qubit-level operations by introducing type systems, annotations or string-based representations that allow programmers to express quantum intent without directly managing gate sequences or hardware layout.
The family includes Silq~\cite{bichsel2020silq}, Q\#~\cite{MicrosoftQSharpOverview}, Qmod~\cite{vax2025qmodexpressivehighlevelquantum}, Quff~\cite{wright2024quff}, ONDA~\cite{degregorio2024onda} and Qutes~\cite{faro2025qutes}.
Each language targets a different point in the design space; taken together, they reveal both the range of strategies available and the structural limits that remain unresolved across the family.

Silq~\cite{bichsel2020silq} introduced automatic uncomputation as the defining contribution of the high-level paradigm.
Temporary ancilla qubits, prepared during computation and ordinarily requiring explicit reversal to avoid entangling the primary register at measurement, are uncomputed by the compiler wherever the type system can guarantee safety~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
The conditions are precise: uncomputation is permitted when the temporary value was produced by a \texttt{qfree} function, one whose semantics can be described classically and when the variables used to produce it remain available~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
Silq encodes these conditions through its annotation system (\texttt{const}, \texttt{qfree}, \texttt{lifted}, \texttt{mfree}), which allows the type-checker to reject programs that would induce implicit measurement without programmer awareness.
The result is a language in which algorithmic structure can be written at a level closer to classical pseudocode, with ancilla management handled implicitly.
Q\#~\cite{MicrosoftQSharpOverview} pursues the same goal through explicit functor annotations (\texttt{Adjoint}, \texttt{Controlled}) that the compiler uses to synthesise inverse and controlled variants of operations, making uncomputation expressible without manual circuit reversal.
Qmod~\cite{vax2025qmodexpressivehighlevelquantum} extends the compiler's reach further, generating executable quantum circuits from high-level algorithmic descriptions while targeting real hardware backends.
These three languages establish a pattern: high-level abstraction is achieved by delegating gate-level decisions to the compiler, with the programmer providing intent through a typed, annotated surface language.

The expressiveness of this approach depends on what the type system can verify statically.
Silq's guarantee holds only for \texttt{lifted} subexpressions; Q\# makes a different tradeoff, with a goal of broad hardware portability over maximal static safety~\cite{MicrosoftQSharpOverview}.
In both cases, the boundaries of safe abstraction are determined by what can be proven at compile time.
Qmod's circuit generation similarly operates within the constraints of what the classical control infrastructure can represent.
This is a structural feature of the paradigm, not an implementation gap: the compiler can only abstract over what it can reason about, and the extent of that reasoning is bounded by the expressiveness of the annotation or type system provided to it.

Quff~\cite{wright2024quff} introduces a dynamically typed system in which the quantum-ness of a value is inferred at runtime rather than declared at compile time; a value becomes quantum-typed when it enters a quantum context, removing the explicit annotation burden present in statically typed alternatives.
Quantum unsigned integers are still initialised with an explicit bit-width, \texttt{0u\{n\}} denoting an $n$-qubit unsigned integer in the zero state, and phase application uses explicit notation (\texttt{Phase(@pi)}).
Function inversion is a first-class runtime operation, \texttt{invertF}, which reverses an arbitrary function over its domain without requiring manual circuit construction; Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:qpl:grover_comparison} illustrates both features.
The requirement to specify qubit counts and explicit phase rotations persists, placing the programmer's mental model at the quantum level for any operation outside the runtime's built-in repertoire.

Qutes~\cite{faro2025qutes} operates at the level of quantum strings.
A \texttt{qustring} aggregates qubits into a named, iterable register.
The \texttt{in} operator performs substring search over that register as a native language construct, hiding the underlying amplitude-based computation entirely, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:qpl:grover_comparison}.
The QFT comparison in Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:qpl:qft_comparison} reveals the limit of this approach: outside the small set of built-in operations, Qutes requires explicit gate instructions,\texttt{ hadamard}, \texttt{mcp} and \texttt{swap} among them, reducing the language to assembly-style circuit construction.
The abstraction is present only where it has been manually encoded as a built-in, not as a general property of the compilation model.

In Quff, the programmer declares bit-widths and the runtime manages gate schedules and inversions.
In Qutes, built-in operations hide quantum structure entirely, but the abstraction is not uniform across the language surface.
Neither language removes the need to reason about the underlying quantum state in the general case; they raise the vocabulary in which that reasoning is expressed at specific, pre-chosen points.
The classical control structure governing when and how quantum operations are applied remains explicitly in the programmer's hands.

ONDA~\cite{degregorio2024onda} occupies a singular position among the languages examined here: where the others depend on a classical coprocessor to manage control flow, ONDA targets a proposed QPU that executes sequentially and branches autonomously.
Control is not absorbed by a classical host; it is encoded directly in the QPU's execution model through an update register \texttt{\$ur} that increments the program counter each cycle and a garbage pointer \texttt{\$grp} that tracks a pre-initialised ancilla region into which temporary values are swapped rather than uncomputed.
Branching is implemented through \texttt{caddi}, a conditional add immediate that adjusts \texttt{\$ur} by a signed offset in response to a condition operand that may itself be a quantum state.
The branch decision is encoded as arithmetic within the QPU rather than delegating it to an external classical processor.

Loops conditioned on quantum data with no classically bounded exit condition are unsound: a loop whose termination depends on a quantum measurement cannot be compiled to a finite circuit~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
ONDA addresses this by requiring the loop exit condition to be a classically determined quantity and\texttt{caddi} advances the program counter past the loop body when that bound is reached.

The garbage register grows monotonically as the computation proceeds.
Temporary values produced by arithmetic operations are swapped into the pre-initialised ancilla region and parked indefinitely; ONDA provides no mechanism for reclaiming them.
The region must be sized to accommodate the worst-case accumulation of the entire program~\cite{degregorio2024onda}.
Silq, Q\# and Quff address the same obligation through automatic uncomputation: reversal is inserted at the point a value leaves scope, on demand via adjoint synthesis, or at function boundaries respectively.
The qubit is reclaimed by reversing the operations that created the temporary~\cite{bichsel2020silq,MicrosoftQSharpOverview, wright2024quff}.
ONDA's swap strategy parks temporaries at negligible circuit cost, but the qubit is never reclaimed; the design trades qubit count for circuit depth, accumulating ancillae across the program rather than reversing them at each use site.

Abstraction level is the highest of any reviewed family: uncomputation, adjoint synthesis and runtime inversion are compiler obligations rather than programmer tasks.
Required quantum knowledge is reduced; programmers using Silq or Quff do not schedule reversals manually, and ONDA confines its quantum-specific vocabulary to three operators.
System portability is where the family is weakest: Silq and Qmod target specific toolchains, Q\# targets the Microsoft quantum stack and ONDA targets a QPU architecture that does not yet exist.
Type restrictions on classical values in superposition, the distinction between adjoint and non-adjoint functions and the injectivity requirement on runtime-inverted operations are the surface manifestations of the\textit{representation exposure} and \textit{semantic mismatch} properties established in Section~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:reading}.
Understanding why they exist is a prerequisite for correct authorship; the programmer's required mental model remains quantum throughout.
ONDA's position in the family is singular: the only proposal that eliminates the classical host dependency at the execution level, it defines what autonomous quantum execution requires at the hardware level and clarifies that the classical control dependency in the other languages reflects the constraints of current hardware rather than a language design choice~\cite{degregorio2024onda}.

\subsection{The Usability Ceiling}\label{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:ceiling}
The four families examined in this section arrive at a consistent limitation: no system reviewed supports problem-space reasoning throughout a complete quantum programming workflow.
Assembly exposes the execution model by design.
Intermediate representations target toolchain authors; their abstraction boundary does not propagate upward to the programmer's experience.
Embedded frameworks lower ergonomic costs without changing the programmer's required mental model.
High-level languages manage specific obligations through type systems and compiler annotations, but the programmer's conceptual vocabulary remains quantum-explicit.
These are not independent shortcomings: they are four manifestations of a shared ceiling.
A survey of 251 active quantum programmers found that 83\% hold at least five years of classical programming experience, yet quantum vocabulary, comprising qubits, gates, measurement mechanics and circuit architecture, remains the primary reported barrier to productive use across all frameworks~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
Thirty-one per cent of those surveyed explicitly identified the need for higher-level languages, and over 40\% reported the language landscape as over-proliferated relative to what the field requires~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
Complexity measurements across language families do not consistently decrease at higher abstraction levels, indicating that abstraction-level labels are not reliable proxies for programmer burden~\cite{corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness}.

The failure modes established in Section~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:reading} account for why this ceiling appears across all four families.
\textit{Representation exposure} manifests wherever an interface exposes qubit types or requires annotations such as \texttt{const}, \texttt{qfree} or \texttt{Adjoint}.
Quantum state cannot be copied or inspected without disturbing the computation, so no module boundary at which quantum and classical code meet can be made quantum-opaque in the way classical interfaces are.
Dynamically typed systems defer rather than eliminate this exposure, surfacing quantum-mechanical constraints as runtime violations rather than static annotations~\cite{wright2024quff}.
\textit{Semantic mismatch} is visible in the type restrictions documented across the high-level family.
The annotation requirements that make safe uncomputation, inversion and controlled-operation synthesis possible are surface evidence of where classically valid operations have no general quantum equivalent~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Both failure modes place the programmer at the quantum substrate regardless of the abstraction level the language claims to occupy.
Applying Wing's definition of computational thinking, the ceiling is the point at which every reviewed system requires the programmer to reason in terms of the quantum machine rather than the problem domain~\cite{Wing2006ComputationalThinking}.

The languages surveyed did not arrive at this ceiling for identical reasons, and not all of those reasons are equally resistant to change.
Automatic uncomputation is the clearest case of a language design problem that has been partially resolved.
The obligation to manually reverse temporary quantum computations, an unavoidable programmer task in early high-level quantum languages, is now managed by the compiler in Silq, Q\# and Qrisp~\cite{bichsel2020silq, MicrosoftQSharpOverview, seidel2024qrisp}.
The annotation requirements that make compiler management possible, \texttt{qfree},\texttt{const} and adjoint constraints, remain visible to the programmer; the obligation has been lifted, but awareness that uncomputation is a quantum-specific concern has not.
Progress in syntactic modularity, evident in Q\#'s operation model and controlled-operation synthesis, has not produced semantic modularity.
Representation exposure prevents module boundaries from being made quantum-opaque in the way classical interfaces are, because any boundary at which a quantum type is passed or returned must expose its quantum-mechanical constraints to the caller~\cite{scott2009plp, yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Compiler-managed obligations have reduced programmer workload without changing the conceptual structure the programmer operates within.
The abstraction analysis establishes that general quantum control flow cannot be faithfully embedded into a circuit model without reference to circuit primitives a result known as the No-Embedding Theorem~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
The annotation requirements that make safe inversion and controlled-operation synthesis possible are its surface manifestation across every high-level family reviewed.
The field's own abstract machine analysis confirms the structural dimension of this picture: no quantum abstract machine candidate satisfies the criteria for symbolic reasoning about operations without reference to circuit primitives.
No production language is built on a substrate that meets those conditions~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
Advancing beyond the current ceiling requires not incremental improvements to existing language designs but a different premise about what the programmer-facing model should contain.

The progress the reviewed languages have made is measurable and confined to specific dimensions.
Where compiler management is available, the programmer is relieved of specifying how quantum state management is performed.
Uncomputation in Silq and Qrisp removes the obligation to manually reverse temporary registers, and automatic inversion via Quff's \texttt{invertF} combinator and Q\#'s \texttt{Adjoint} functor synthesis removes the obligation to specify how a quantum operation is reversed~\cite{bichsel2020silq, seidel2024qrisp,wright2024quff, MicrosoftQSharpOverview}.
These advances reduce a specific source of cognitive overhead without requiring the programmer to reason at the circuit level for the covered cases~\cite{corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness}.
The type systems of Silq and Q\# formalise the semantic mismatch boundary, converting a class of runtime errors into compile-time guarantees; the mismatch itself is not resolved, but its consequences are made explicit and checkable~\cite{bichsel2020silq, MicrosoftQSharpOverview}.
Hardware portability in Q\# and PennyLane's differentiable circuit integration address structural concerns, porting cost and hybrid workflow management, that are distinct from the conceptual vocabulary barrier~\cite{MicrosoftQSharpOverview,bergholm2018pennylane}.
None of these advances address the primary barrier identified in surveys of quantum programmers: quantum vocabulary, comprising qubits, gates and measurement, remains the required conceptual currency across every reviewed system~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
The ceiling has not moved; the obligations within it have been partially rationalised.

The gap between what the reviewed languages offer and what quantum programming productivity requires was identified in the literature two decades before the systems surveyed here were built.
A survey of quantum programming language design published in 2006 identified the absence of complex data structure abstractions and expressive quantum control constructs as the primary missing elements for raising quantum programming above the circuit level~\cite{Gay2006QPLSurvey}.
Neither gap has been closed in the systems reviewed in this section.
Recent practical experience with quantum programming corroborates the assessment: requiring programmers to construct bespoke quantum circuits for each problem class has been compared to the 1940s and 1950s model of physical computer reconfiguration.
Each new task required hardware rewiring rather than software specification~\cite{cobb2022higherlevelabstractions}.

Theoretical work on quantum abstraction demonstrates that the data structure gap is not necessarily permanent.
A quantum data structure model can be constructed to satisfy three properties required for safe abstract use: \textit{history independence}, where observable behaviour does not depend on allocation history; \textit{reversibility}, where all operations can be undone; and \textit{bounded-time execution}, where operation costs are independent of internal state~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Such a structure can be used correctly without reasoning about its internal quantum state.
It therefore satisfies the precondition for a data abstraction tier that does not expose quantum-mechanical vocabulary at the programmer interface.
Every family reviewed in this section requires circuit-level reasoning at specific workflow points consistent with the finding that no existing abstract machine substrate separates instruction symbols from circuit primitives~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
Qutes demonstrates both the promise and the limit of approaching this tier through built-in algorithm abstractions: Grover's algorithm is completely hidden behind an ordinary string operation, but any algorithm outside the small set of built-in constructs requires direct gate instructions at the source level~\cite{faro2025qutes}.
Quff advances furthest on vocabulary reduction through dynamic type inference, while Silq provides the strongest compile-time safety guarantees against quantum-mechanical misuse~\cite{wright2024quff,bichsel2020silq}.
None provides the data structure model or quantum control constructs that a language occupying this absent tier would require.
A programming layer at this tier would supply abstract data types whose interfaces do not expose qubit-level mechanics.
Quantum control constructs would not require circuit-level specification, and module boundaries would not propagate quantum-mechanical constraints to their callers.
These are the conditions under which programmers without quantum-mechanical expertise could reason about quantum algorithms using classical software engineering concepts.
What such a layer would require of a language design is the question Chapter~\ref{chapt:pub_language_design} addresses.
