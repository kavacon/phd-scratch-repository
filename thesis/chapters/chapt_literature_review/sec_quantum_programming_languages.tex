\section{Quantum Programming Languages}\label{sec:lit_rev:qpl}
Programming languages are the tool with which computer scientists and software engineers convey their algorithmic intent to machines for execution.
As described in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction} programming languages come in many types and families, each serving a different role within the execution stack or compilation chain.
These roles include high-level languages, designed to make human intent as easy as possible to express, intermediate representations which make program structure explicit for compiler analysis and transformation and assembly languages which describe execution in terms machines directly operate on.

This section explores the current state of quantum programming languages; those intended to execute on quantum computers.
It examines which layers of the compilation chain are represented, what they offer and whether they support the expression of human intent without requiring programmers to reason at the level of the quantum machine.
The evidence shows genuine progress across all layers; no existing system, however, enables sustained human-centred reasoning: the programmer's required mental model remains machine-centred throughout.

\subsection{Scope and Vocabulary}\label{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:scope}
The review presented in this section will analyse quantum programming languages across four categories or families.
Each of those families is described in Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:qpl:language_families}.
The definitions of these families, their taxonomy and critical analyses are expanded in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:taxonomy}.
Other frameworks assess quantum languages on their semantic or syntactic principles, such as imperative and functional languages~\cite{Gay2006QPLSurvey}.
The review presented here does not do that and instead focuses on the intended purpose in the compilation stack.

Each family represents a different role in the compilation and execution of quantum programs or provides ergonomic or abstraction benefits.
The families of languages selected represent languages that are used by programmers in the development of quantum programs or those required to demonstrate the full compilation chains and comparisons of complexity.
From those criteria languages eligible for inclusion are embedded frameworks in classical languages, intermediate representation languages, quantum assembly languages and high-level quantum development languages.
Languages that solely represent hardware control and vendor or specific machine level languages are excluded from review as they are either not intended for human development or fail to add value to discussions about abstraction.

To evaluate the current state of quantum programming languages, the review will consider the following elements of each language and family:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Abstraction level: The degree to which a system exposes machine-level quantum detail to a programmer~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy}.
    \item Required quantum knowledge: What quantum mechanical understanding a programmer must have to productively use the language~\cite{Myers2016PLUsabilitySIG, ferreira2024qplusage}.
    \item System portability: Whether the language can be used to target multiple hardware backends without programmer intervention~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy, nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
\end{itemize}
Together these criteria represent existing evaluation frameworks and known areas of friction for practitioners of quantum programming.
The level to which a language adheres to them is a signifier of its ability to advance the current state of quantum programming languages.

Each language family will have a different aspirational level for the criteria presented, intermediate representations will require a higher degree of quantum knowledge than high-level languages.
The criteria are applied contextually based on the purpose of each language family.

\subsection{Taxonomy and the Missing Tier}\label{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:taxonomy}
Sections~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:levels_elements} and~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:looking_forward} established that quantum programming lacks a machine-independent source layer.
This section grounds that finding in an examination of what each language family aspires to and whether any family achieves it.

The defining aspiration of quantum programming language development is quantum-agnostic application development~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
A family of languages that fulfills this aspiration would not require fundamental knowledge of quantum mechanics or quantum circuits to program.
Variations of this aspiration exist; some argue that quantum agnosticism could be achieved by improving circuit generation techniques to remove quantum pre-requisite knowledge~\cite{cobb2022higherlevelabstractions}.
Others identify a lack of separation between instructions and circuit primitives as preventing the formulation of a genuine source-layer language~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
Additional analysis has shown a lack of complex data structures and quantum control sequence support~\cite{Gay2006QPLSurvey} as further barriers.

Regardless of the path, the literature converges on fundamental principles such a language family would adhere to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Minimal or no understanding of fundamental quantum mechanics required to write programs.
    \item No direct manipulation of the quantum circuit model or equivalents in other hardware.
    \item The programmers mental model exists in the problem space; not quantum mechanics or circuits.
\end{itemize}

Four language families are identified in the literature and introduced in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:scope}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item High-level
    \item Embedded framework
    \item Intermediate representation
    \item Assembly
\end{itemize}

Each offers different aspirational goals in the quantum programming domain.
A summary is provided in Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:qpl:language_family_taxonomy}.
This subsection characterises each family; Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:reading} examines them in detail.

Assembly languages such as OpenQASM aspire to offer a portable, hardware-agnostic specification of quantum circuits.
They describe circuit primitives alongside hardware concerns such as pulses, timing delays and qubit rotations or gates~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
OpenQASM achieves these aspirations.
Quantum assembly languages differ fundamentally from their classical analogues; they don't describe microprocessor instructions, they describe circuits.
Quantum assembly languages at their most fundamental are a mathematical abstraction over circuit construction, offering hardware independent ways to describe that structure.
While they do offer lower level control such as pulses and timing, predominantly \textit{how} to physically realise a logical gate is left up to toolchains specific to the hardware.
Classical assembly languages such as RISC or x86 conversely describe instructions given to hardware but have no mathematical description of how those instructions are physically built in a circuit.

Intermediate representations (IRs) offer the same aspirations for quantum programming as their classical counterparts.
A productive IR is machine-independent and stable across the compilation stack; both forward and backward compatible~\cite{irreview2024}.
IRs retain the intent of the source layer while enabling compiler optimisation passes and formal verification~\cite{irreview2024}.
Current quantum IRs may or may not encode quantum-physical constraints such as no-cloning or teleportation.
QIR and Q-MLIR achieve these outcomes~\cite{irreview2024}.

Embedded frameworks enable ecosystem integration for remote execution and simulation of quantum programs from within a classical programming language, providing ergonomic tools for common quantum algorithms and circuit compilation for quantum execution systems.
Qiskit~\cite{aleksandrowicz2019qiskit} is a representative example, offering Python libraries for circuit construction, compilation across multiple hardware backends and remote execution.
PennyLane~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane} offers comparable tooling with a focus on variational quantum circuits and machine learning techniques.

High-level languages aspire to provide a layer where algorithmic thinking drives the programmer.
These languages aim to provide an abstraction level where the compiler can reason about quantum obligations such as physical correctness and resource management.
As Section~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:in_quantum_computing} showed, Silq, Qmod and Q\# offer many high-level tools to support algorithmic programming.
However, many of these tools still require the programmer to understand the quantum-mechanical and circuit-level principles underlying their programs.
High-level languages do not divorce the quantum from quantum programming.

The taxonomy reveals a missing ``fifth-tier'' of quantum programming languages.
The quantum-agnostic, application programming paradigm aspired to in the literature is not realisable with any of the language families presented.
Assembly and intermediate representations are explicitly quantum by design, requiring a fundamental understanding of circuit primitives and hardware concerns.
Embedded frameworks were not intended to produce quantum-agnosticism; their goal is to improve access to near-term quantum computers and remote execution of quantum circuit definitions.
Embedded frameworks achieve this effectively, but it is not an adequate solution to the missing tier.
High-level languages come closest, but their fundamental coupling to quantum circuit primitives and resource management requires the programmer to have a quantum mechanical mental model.
These languages do not enable problems and solutions to be formulated purely algorithmically.

\subsection{Reading the Language Landscape}\label{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:reading}
Quantum programming language development continues to focus on improving computability and tractability within quantum domains rather than focusing on improving the experience of programmers~\cite{corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness}.
Assessing programming languages solely against their computability biases success towards machine concerns rather than human factors and programmer experience.

Current quantum programming languages suffer from a usability issue failing to be addressed at a fundamental level~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage, nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
A survey of active quantum programming projects on GitHub showed a lack of novel development or algorithmic investigation~\cite{murillo2022sequantumprogramming}.
Surveys of embedded framework usage found that their popularity can be attributed to the improved programmer experience when using a classical language; however, programmers still struggled with the circuit architecture~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
Programmers consistently report quantum domain knowledge, vocabulary and concepts as barriers to entry and understanding of quantum languages~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage, murillo2022sequantumprogramming}.

Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:scope} established three criteria that can be used to critically examine quantum programming languages, these criteria are directly related to the programmer experience:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Abstraction level.
    \item Required quantum knowledge.
    \item System portability.
\end{itemize}

High-level programming languages realise the abstraction requirements described in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction}.
In classical programming language theory they provide a control plane that reduces a programmer's exposure to machine concerns~\cite{scott2009plp}.

In the remainder of this subsection, the various language families in quantum computing are described in detail.
Their definitions are presented and members of the family analysed for their features and success as programming languages.
Languages are assessed on the degree to which they support computational thinking paradigms~\cite{Wing2006ComputationalThinking}, graded based on their family and position in the compilation stack.

Each language family is assessed on its suitability to support the ideal quantum abstract machine~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines} described in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction} with respect to its role in compilation.
High-level languages are assessed with additional rigour against their ability to shield programmers from inherent constraints in quantum programming not present in classical programming.
\textit{Representation exposure}; the degree to which a programmer is required to reason about internal quantum state directly, due to the inability to copy or inspect it without disturbing the computation.
\textit{Semantic mismatch}; the degree to which invalid equivalence with classical concepts is assumed~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.

\subsubsection{Assembly Languages}\label{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:assembly}
Assembly languages describe quantum computation at the level of the execution model: explicit instructions over named quantum resources, applied in a sequence that hardware can directly interpret.
OpenQASM is the canonical representative of this family~\cite{cross2017openqasm, cross2022openqasm3}.

OpenQASM states its primary goal is to serve as an intermediate representation for communicating with quantum hardware, with allowances made for human usability~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
The language describes quantum circuits using explicit qubit register declarations, named gate operations and measurement instructions.
Gates are drawn from the CNOT-plus-SU(2) basis: the controlled-NOT gate, which entangles two qubits, and the special unitary group SU(2), which encompasses all single-qubit rotations; together these are enough for universal quantum computation~\cite{cross2017openqasm}.

Version 3.0 extended the language with classical types (\texttt{int}, \texttt{float}, \texttt{bool}, \texttt{angle}), control flow constructs (\texttt{if}/\texttt{while}/\texttt{for}), real-time feed-forward and explicit timing and scheduling directives~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
An optional pulse-level calibration mechanism, introduced via the \texttt{defcal} keyword and OpenPulse grammar, allows gate implementations to be overridden with hardware-specific microwave pulse sequences applied directly to qubits, extending the language's reach below the circuit model when required~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
Like classical assembly languages, which provide a symbolic, near-one-to-one representation of machine instructions~\cite{scott2009plp}, OpenQASM presents quantum operations in terms that correspond directly to what the underlying hardware performs.
Portability is achieved through a shared vocabulary of quantum operations: hardware-specific gate sets and timing constraints are resolved downstream, not at the language level.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:qpl:assembly_comparison} the assembly label, while conventional, overstates the analogy with classical computing.
Classical assembly converges programmer vocabulary with hardware execution: a MOV or ADD instruction corresponds directly to what the processor does.
OpenQASM, by contrast, describes logical gate primitives that are hardware-independent by design; the physical realisation; pulse shaping, basis gate decomposition, timing calibration; lives below it in vendor toolchains the programmer never touches.
The quantum programmer-facing model is therefore removed from the specific hardware, yet the full cognitive burden of circuit-level reasoning is still a hard requirement.

In practice, OpenQASM programs are authored by compilers and toolchains rather than human programmers directly.
Evaluating OpenQASM using the framework established in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:qpl:reading} can be achieved by analysing a representative workflow; the analysis surfaces the abstraction level and quantum knowledge requirements the language imposes.

Creating a superposition and conditioning later execution on a measurement outcome is one such workflow.
To achieve that workflow, the programmer must declare a qubit register, apply a Hadamard gate (\texttt{h q}) to create superposition, collect a measurement outcome into a classical bit register and write a control flow conditioned on that result as described in Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:qpl:open_qasm_example}.
At no point does the language offer a layer at which this workflow can be expressed without direct reference to qubits, gates and measurement mechanics: the circuit is the expression of thought.

The classical control flow introduced in version 3.0 merits specific attention because it is presented as evidence of abstraction-level progress~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
The syntax is C-like: typed variables, \texttt{while} loops, \texttt{if}/\texttt{else} constructs and familiar operator precedence.
A programmer arriving from classical software development will recognise these constructs immediately.
What the syntax does not expose is the constraint on their semantics: classical operations in OpenQASM execute on a real-time quantum controller, bounded by the feed-forward window imposed by qubit coherence times; operations that cannot complete within this window must be delegated to \texttt{extern} functions, which are opaque to the OpenQASM layer and executed by an external classical processor~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
The specification acknowledges this directly: the language is not designed for general-purpose classical computation~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.
The C-like syntax provides coordination scaffolding for quantum circuits; it does not provide a general classical programming model, and it leaves the quantum substrate entirely unchanged.

The structural position of OpenQASM provides insight into the difficulties being faced with improved abstraction in quantum computing~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
Classical abstraction could progressively absorb hardware detail because the lowest programmer concepts were already close to the machine~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
The quantum stack conceptually begins by conflating mathematical circuit description with low-level programming
Abstracting away from it requires not just hiding hardware detail but replacing a circuit-oriented mental model entirely; a qualitatively greater undertaking.

OpenQASM achieves what it sets out to do: it provides a portable, complete execution-level specification that functions as a hardware-independent circuit interchange format.
Version 3.0 is a richer format than its predecessor.
In classical programming language theory, however, raising a language's abstraction level means reducing the programmer's required exposure to machine-level concerns~\cite{scott2009plp}; adding features that expand expressiveness without reducing that exposure does not constitute a change of level.
The programmer using OpenQASM, regardless of version, must reason in terms of qubit registers, gate operations and measurement outcomes.
The language's position in the compilation stack is fixed by design intent, not by implementation immaturity.

\subsubsection{Intermediate Representations}\label{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:ir}
Intermediate representations occupy a distinct role in any compilation stack: they are machine-independent substrates designed not for human authorship, but for compiler analysis, optimisation and transformation~\cite{scott2009plp, irreview2024}.
In classical programming language theory, an IR sits between the source layer and assembly; high-level source code is lowered into an IR, optimisation passes are applied and the result is lowered again into assembly for hardware execution~\cite{scott2009plp}.
Two representative quantum IRs are examined here.
Quantum Intermediate Representation (QIR) is an LLVM-based standard developed by the QIR Alliance~\cite{qiralliance_spec}.
Q-MLIR is a family of MLIR-based quantum dialects enabling progressive lowering through multiple compiler stages~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
Both languages claim to offer a common substrate enabling multiple front-end languages, reusable compiler passes and a formal verification surface across the quantum software stack~\cite{irreview2024}.

As with assembly, the IR label implies a classical relationship that does not hold in the quantum stack.
In classical computing, IR sits \textit{above} assembly in the pipeline; it is the layer that assembly is compiled \textit{from}.
In quantum computing the pipeline inverts: OpenQASM programs are parsed into MLIR quantum dialect representations, progressively lowered to LLVM IR adherent to QIR specifications and transformed into backend-specific executable code~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
Quantum IR therefore sits \textit{below} quantum assembly in the compilation pipeline, a position with no direct classical analogue.
In classical stacks there is nothing below assembly except machine code, which is an encoding concern rather than a programmable layer.
This inversion compounds the structural asymmetry identified in Section~\ref{subsubsec:lit_rev:qpl:assembly}: the quantum programmer-facing floor is already more abstract than classical assembly relative to the hardware, and below it lies a rich IR infrastructure that classical abstraction journeys never encountered.

QIR is constructed as an extension of LLVM IR, the foundational intermediate representation of the classical LLVM compiler infrastructure~\cite{scott2009plp}.
Where LLVM IR represents values as typed references that classical code can read,write and pass freely, QIR introduces two dedicated abstract resource types: one for qubits and one for measurement results~\cite{qiralliance_spec}.
These types are defined without any internal structure accessible to the programmer; classical code cannot read, copy, or directly manipulate them, and all interactions must go through a defined set of quantum operations.
This restriction encodes a foundational quantum constraint at the IR layer: qubits are physical resources that cannot be duplicated or inspected without disturbing their state.
All quantum operations in QIR are declared as external functions whose implementations are deferred to hardware backends.
The IR specifies \textit{that} an operation exists and what resources it acts on, but not \textit{how} it is physically realised, allowing the same QIR program to target diverse hardware without modification~\cite{qiralliance_spec}.
An explicit resource lifecycle model tracks when qubits are acquired and released.
Hooks are provided for detecting unsafe patterns such as operating on a qubit after it has been freed, analogous to memory safety analysis in classical compilation~\cite{qiralliance_spec, qirformalization2024}.

A more fundamental limitation is that QIR's specification remains informal, described in natural language without formal semantics~\cite{qirformalization2024}.
Formalisation efforts~\cite{qirformalization2024} are themselves motivated by this gap, noting that the absence of formal definitions creates interpretational ambiguity when implementing compilation toolchains.
A concrete illustration of this instability is a known incompatibility between QIR's typed resource handles and a change introduced in LLVM 15, which replaced all typed pointer representations with a single generic pointer type; this conflict remains unresolved in the QIR specification~\cite{qiralliance_spec}.
Taken together, these findings position QIR as a promising but incomplete substrate whose verification guarantees are real but not yet fully realised.

Q-MLIR approaches the IR problem through a different architectural strategy: rather than a single standardised target format, it provides a framework for building domain-specific compiler pipelines using MLIR's dialect mechanism~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
A dialect defines a set of operations specific to a domain, each with a name, typed inputs and outputs and compile-time attributes; conversion rules specify how operations in one dialect are progressively rewritten into another until a common hardware-compatible base is reached.
A representative quantum dialect provides a minimal operation set: register allocation and deallocation, qubit addressing within a register, and general gate application~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
A key design feature is the use of static single assignment (SSA) form, in which each value is defined exactly once and consumed explicitly.
SSA applied to quantum resources makes dataflow visible to the compiler and allows constraints such as the no cloning theorem to be enforced structurally rather than by convention~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
Q-MLIR and QIR are linked through compilation.
MLIR dialects provide the multi-level lowering infrastructure.
QIR serves as the standardised LLVM-compatible target MLIR lowers into.
MLIR's built-in LLVM dialect provides established conversion routines, so a quantum dialect need only define rewrite rules from its own operations to LLVM primitives and the remaining lowering path is inherited~\cite{McCaskey2021MLIRDialectQASM}.
The viability of this approach in production toolchains is demonstrated by Catalyst~\cite{PennyLaneCatalystMLIRDialectsDocs}, PennyLane's MLIR-based compiler.
Catalyst lowers differentiable quantum programs through a custom quantum dialect pipeline to hardware-executable targets, illustrating how the dialect mechanism scales from prototype to industrial deployment.

Comparative assessment places both systems similarly: high ratings for hardware agnosticism and optimisation capability, low ratings for tooling accessibility and ease of use~\cite{irreview2024}.
A shared structural limitation reinforces this picture: neither system can distinguish qubits on the same processor from those on separate networked nodes.
Distributed quantum computation is unrepresentable at the IR layer~\cite{irreview2024}.
Proposed extensions~\cite{cardama2024netqir} address this by introducing communication primitives that abstract internode state transfer, mirroring how classical distributed IRs such as OpenCL approach heterogeneous compute.
This suggests that compatibility with classical IR infrastructure is a more productive path than independent reinvention.

The IR family sits below quantum assembly and above hardware execution in the compilation stack, and its intended audience is compiler and toolchain authors rather than application programmers.
Applying the computational thinking framework established in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:abstraction}, the IR layer does not support problem-space mental models.
The contribution of IRs is enabling stable, reusable transformations between layers, not reducing the programmer's required quantum vocabulary.
The IR layer is providing the abstraction boundary its design intends: a stable, hardware-agnostic substrate that absorbs low-level concerns below the programmer-facing stack~\cite{irreview2024, qiralliance_spec}.
Whether the layers above it consistently exploit that boundary is explored in discussions on Embedded Frameworks and High level languages.
