\section{Abstraction}\label{sec:lit_rev:abstraction}
Abstraction is a fundamental concept in computer science and software engineering, providing the basis for tooling and development in both fields.
Abstraction is equally fundamental as a mode of thinking in other domains, describing an approach that is useful for separating concerns and reducing complexity~\cite{Wing2006ComputationalThinking}.
Examples of this cross-domain application appear in fields such as mathematics in abstract algebra, physics with effective field theory~\cite{Burgess2020EFT} and linguistics with Chomsky's grammar hierarchy~\cite{Chomsky1957SyntacticStructures}.

Good abstraction provides practitioners of any field with the ability to remove complexity without losing expressivity in the solutions they can explore and produce~\cite{parnas1972criteria,aho2022abstractions}.
A well-designed abstraction reduces complexity for its user through well-defined interfaces, without constraining the space of solutions they can express or construct~\cite{parnas1972criteria}.

As this section demonstrates, quantum computing has not yet realised the abstraction quality that characterises mature classical programming systems: existing approaches improve ergonomics but largely operate at the functional level, leaving correctness, portability and conceptual expressivity underserved.
Establishing a principled, concrete definition of abstraction and a framework for differentiating abstraction levels is therefore a prerequisite for any rigorous evaluation of quantum programming systems.
This section develops that baseline, drawing on classical computing literature, and applies it to characterise the current state of abstraction in quantum computing.

The resulting framework is carried forward throughout the remainder of this literature review and underpins the critical analysis of quantum programming language abstractions presented in Chapter~\ref{chapt:pub_language_design}.

\subsection{Defining Abstraction}\label{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:defining_abstraction}
While abstraction is useful both within and beyond computer science, this subsection focuses on its definition specifically in computer science and software engineering.
The aim is to present the ways in which abstraction is defined and discussed in the literature and to identify the roles and uses of different definitions.
These definitions ground the discussion throughout the remainder of this section.
The focus is predominantly on literature published since 2020, though older works establishing standard interpretations of foundational concepts are drawn upon where appropriate.

Abstraction as used in this subsection may refer to either a layered system with reduced or increased complexity between layers or to a specific layer within such a system.
This discussion distinguishes the two using the terms \textit{System (of abstraction)} and \textit{Abstraction (layer)} respectively.
Regardless of which form is under discussion, any system of abstraction and the layers within it share a common set of defining elements:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Decomposition of complexity into generalised tractable domains: assumptions delimit the scope of the system, allowing complexity to be managed without exhaustive enumeration~\cite{Wing2006ComputationalThinking}.
    \item Presence of data models and operations over them: the system defines what it acts on and how~\cite{aho2022abstractions}.
    \item A mechanism for transitioning between more and less general layers: complexity can be recovered or removed as required~\cite{aho2022abstractions}.
\end{itemize}
These three criteria are present in any system of abstraction.

The presence of greater generality or more assumptions is commonly termed \textit{higher level}; lesser generality and greater specificity, \textit{lower level}.
In computer science, compilation refers to transitions between levels of generality within a system of abstractions~\cite{aho2022abstractions}.

Higher- to lower-level relationships also arise between distinct systems of abstraction, where a higher-level system is grounded in or augmented by a lower-level description of the same domain.
An example of this relationship between abstraction systems is the three-layered model of parallel computing~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy}, comprising the \textbf{Programming}, \textbf{Execution} and \textbf{Hardware} layers.
Each layer makes assumptions about the availability of operations in the layer below, thereby increasing its own generality; Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:abstraction:abstraction_relationships} describes these dependency relationships.

Additional specificity or generality can be introduced on either side of this layered structure: at the more general end, all layers may collapse into a single bridging model~\cite{Valiant1990BSP} used as its own layer in higher-level systems.
At the more specific end, the programming layer may be expanded to include high-level languages such as Python or domain specifications for machine learning.

The scope of any system of abstraction is therefore contingent on the discussion it is designed to support; the definitions established here are applied with that flexibility in mind.

It is necessary to be able to compare both systems of abstraction and abstractions to determine their applicability to a given domain and value in a situation.
Comparative relationships between abstractions are well documented; however, formalisms for discussing such relationships between systems of abstraction are absent from the literature.
The taxonomy of abstractions~\cite{aho2022abstractions} provides a spectrum from Fundamental → Abstract → Declarative → Computational, offering a framework for describing relationships between abstractions in computational processes.
Through this taxonomy it is possible to relate abstractions at higher or lower levels to each other, forming systems of abstractions.
As an example, Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:abstraction:abstraction_taxonomy} illustrates the taxonomic relationships of dictionaries and maps~\cite{aho2022abstractions}.

Comparative frameworks such as the taxonomy are useful for comparing abstractions but provide no real measure of comparison between systems.
Two complementary definitions are introduced here that will allow us to discuss comparisons between more and less abstract systems.
A system could simultaneously belong to both definitions based on its position within the spectrum of a specific domain, as we saw with parallel computing.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Conceptual abstraction}: An abstraction characterised by a small number of broad, inherent concepts that define its operational scope.
    \item \textit{Functional abstraction}: A hierarchy of abstractions forming layers with distinct implementation roles.
\end{itemize}

These definitions provide conceptual tools for comparing abstractions, but do not suffice for critically examining the state of abstraction in quantum computing addressed in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:in_quantum_computing}.
The taxonomy describes abstraction across computation broadly and within systems; it does not provide the vocabulary to locate languages within a compilation stack specifically.
The system comparison framework likewise allows us to reason about the applicability of different systems within a domain but does not define which system configurations serve compilation or computation.
More precise vocabulary is therefore required that can:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Define systems of abstraction for programming language compilation.
    \item Categorise and place languages within systems of abstraction.
\end{itemize}

These tools will allow us to understand the roles of different quantum programming languages discussed in Section~\ref{sec:lit_rev:quantum_programming_languages} and develop the assessment frameworks in Chapter~\ref{chapt:pub_language_design}.
To arrive at our required definitions, we first need to explore the ways in which abstraction is used in programming.

The journey to define abstraction within programming and computer science has been ongoing since the 20th century.
The development of higher level programming languages and increasingly complex systems required definitions to delineate how and when abstraction should and could be used.

Abstraction in programming defines more than just how languages within a compilation stack relate to each other, it also defines how systems are designed and modularised.
The language used to describe both is rooted in the same general principles:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Abstraction hides information and defines boundaries of responsibilities~\cite{parnas1972criteria}.
    \item Abstraction preserves semantic intention between layers~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
    \item Abstraction improves a user's ability to reason with the layer of a system~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy}.
\end{itemize}

In programming languages abstraction defines the responsibility of a language to both the user and the machine.
The system of abstraction selected for a given domain must responsibly hide information at each layer without inhibiting the user's ability to express their intent.
Simultaneously it must ensure that intention is preserved so that the machine can execute it faithfully.

But abstraction also defines the limitations of control a user of a language has on the system they are manipulating~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Abstraction in programming removes responsibility over details or actions that can be assumed or hidden, which also removes the ability to control them.
A canonical example in classical programming is the presence or absence of garbage collection in a given language.

Now can it be said that Java is a higher abstraction of C given one implements garbage collection and the other doesn't?
Most definitions of abstraction in programming would acknowledge Java is higher-level but wouldn't necessarily place it at a higher \textit{abstraction layer} within a programming system.
Why is that?

Abstraction in programming is defined by more than just information hiding or reduction in control.
In programming intention influences abstraction, the intention of a language in concert with the actions or control it permits is what places it at a certain layer in a system.
Languages like C or Java sit at the same layer in parallel systems of abstraction because they inhabit the same intention, for example, application development.
It's for this reason we don't look at C as being an assembly language despite its ability to have low-level control over memory; that's not its intended purpose.

The preceding discussion motivates the following definitions, which are used throughout this review.

\textit{Language abstractions} are user-facing representations that hide implementation details from an end user.
Implementation details may include specifics for interfacing with a machine, physical system properties or operations whose behaviour can be inferred from the domain context.
A language abstraction promotes tractability within a domain by improving a user's ability to reason about the behaviour of programs in that domain.
A well-formed language abstraction preserves expressivity within the domain, even where it constrains execution-level control or reduces access to low-level implementation choices.
For example, a user need not specify the underlying character array representation of a string yet retain full expressive capacity for string manipulation.

\textit{Compiler chains} define computational systems of abstraction.
Compiler chains link high-level to low-level language abstractions by translating from more to less general representations.
A well-formed compiler chain guarantees no loss of intention across layers; higher layers are expected to reduce the cognitive burden on the programmer.
Compiler chains can be compared faithfully by expressing the same system intention in their layers.

The following section examines how these chains have been realised in classical computing systems, establishing a comparative baseline for evaluating quantum programming abstractions later in this review.

\subsection{Levels and elements of abstraction}\label{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:levels_elements}
Abstraction is defined in multiple ways as shown in Section~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:defining_abstraction}.
Abstraction can be defined relatively between layers in a system using the taxonomy of abstraction~\cite{aho2022abstractions} or relatively between systems.
In computation abstraction is defined both as languages that inhabit layers and compiler chains that describe systems of layers.
This subsection focuses specifically on those systems.

Examples will be presented of different ways of organising abstraction within classical computing domains and a framework for assessing the effectiveness of those abstractions provided.
Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:abstraction:classical_compiler_chains} provides a graphical representation illustrating three chains of compilation within classical systems.
The first is one most software engineers and programmers would be familiar with; C may be replaced with many high-level languages and the structure would still hold.
The C chain describes a common separation of layers in programming abstraction with programming, execution and hardware layers.
The other two examples build on this with the SQL-led chain showing an extended compiler chain with a domain specification language (DSL) included.
The Python chain provides a discussion point.

Based on Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:abstraction:classical_compiler_chains} Python is serving as a machine learning (ML) DSL as it has come to be used in recent years~\cite{agrawal2019tensorflow}.
However, Python can also be a scripting or programming layer; this is an example of how the compilation chains are flexible based on the intention of a layer.
What matters here is the audience of the abstraction and how the choice of abstraction benefits or hinders their ability to interact with it~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy}.

When Python acts as an ML DSL, it provides the purpose of hiding common programming paradigms and simplifying the way ML engineers and data scientists interact with machines.
SQL serves a similar purpose allowing non-programmers to interact with computational systems in a way that benefits their domain and level of understanding.
What counts as the highest abstraction level is relative to the domain and intended audience; in both cases presented the audience loses control over memory management or algorithmic decisions.
Despite these losses, the information they hide allows the user to experience a more tractable version of computation in line with their understanding.

The success of classical compiler chains is not accidental; it is grounded in the choice of abstract machine that underlies them.
The von Neumann stored-program model succeeded not because it faithfully modelled the hardware executing it, but because its mental model; sequential instructions over addressable memory; aligned with human problem-solving idioms~\cite{elgotrobinson1964raspm}.
It was that alignment that enabled the later proliferation of high-level languages; Fortran, ALGOL, COBOL each building a different surface over the same foundation.
Abelson and Sussman capture the principle precisely: ``programs are meant to be read by humans and only incidentally meant for computers to execute''~\cite{abelson1996sicp}.

While these layers appear well-structured, whether these compilation chains are effective needs to be qualified.
Good abstraction must match the intention of the definition outlined in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:defining_abstraction}.
To recap, that means:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Abstraction hides information and defines boundaries of responsibilities~\cite{parnas1972criteria}.
    \item Abstraction preserves semantic intention between layers~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
    \item Abstraction improves a user's ability to reason with the layer of a system~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy}.
\end{itemize}

Effective abstractions guarantee that definition and can be assessed by the following criteria:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Correctness of execution can be verified through conditions and invariants~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions, LiskovGuttag1986}.
    \item Data types define what they do, not how they do it~\cite{LiskovGuttag1986}.
    \item Procedures have formal, guaranteed definitions~\cite{LiskovGuttag1986}.
    \item Produced solutions reduce in complexity in higher layers~\cite{aho2022abstractions,DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy}.
    \item Layers can be internally changed without \textit{requiring} changes in other layers~\cite{parnas1972criteria}.
\end{itemize}

Understanding what an abstraction is and how to assess it provides an insight into why abstraction exists and its role in simplifying complex systems.
What these concepts do not provide is commentary on why it is important for the users of those systems or why it matters to the design of quantum computing languages.

The criteria established here, information hiding, semantic preservation, formal definition and complexity reduction will serve as the evaluative baseline when comparing quantum programming systems in the sections that follow.

In Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:why_matters} a review concerning the importance of traditional and non-traditional abstraction systems is presented.
The review provides qualitative results on why it is important to continue to examine abstraction in emerging systems and the benefits it provides their users.

\subsection{Why abstraction matters}\label{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:why_matters}
The properties of abstraction established in Subsections~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:defining_abstraction} and~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:levels_elements}: information hiding, modularity and layered complexity reduction, are foundational to understanding why abstraction matters in practice.
Why abstraction matters really boils down to its ability to improve tractability of domains, to reduce complex problem spaces and map them to more manageable equivalents, and the ability of users to reason with a problem space, in and outside of computer science~\cite{Wing2006ComputationalThinking}.
Where an abstraction fails to deliver this simplification, it fails to benefit its users.

Traditional compiler chain abstractions discussed in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:levels_elements} are an easy example of that drive for improved tractability in a domain.
Examining this more closely shows evidence that as abstractions become higher-level, they provide both comprehension gains and efficiency gains for developers~\cite{Prechelt2000EmpiricalComparison}.
When presented with the same programming task, the use of a higher-level scripting language such as Python has been shown to half the time required to generate a working solution while also halving the size of that solution in terms of lines of code written~\cite{Prechelt2000EmpiricalComparison}.
Comparatively in lower-level imperative or conventional languages, programmers are slower to produce a working solution and require more verbosity to do so~\cite{Prechelt2000EmpiricalComparison}.

The cognitive science basis for these gains is well established: human working memory can sustain only a limited number of simultaneous concerns~\cite{miller1956magicalnumber}.
Abstraction is the mechanism by which programming systems manage that bound, reducing the number of details a programmer must hold in mind at any one time.

In this example the outcome of successful abstraction in the form of higher level languages promotes a reduction in cognitive burden on the user.
An equivalent solution is producible more quickly and more succinctly, which indicates less time to reason with a domain and understand the available solution space.
Abstraction matters because it promotes improved tractability even in unfamiliar or new problem areas.

The development of natural language interfaces for structured data querying is another example of the benefits abstraction provides to tractability and access.
SQL is a well-known domain query language for relational databases which enabled the querying of large data schemas without the need to re-implement search algorithms or data handling, this was pointed out as part of a domain specification abstraction in Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:abstraction:classical_compiler_chains}.
SQL itself may simplify or abstract the way users interact with the writing or re-use of search algorithms, but it presents its own challenges by introducing a new, specific, domain language that potential users must learn to use and understand~\cite{IpeirotisZheng2025NLIDBUsers}.
In the last decades evidence has mounted that natural language interfaces can lower the burden of entry for novice users of relational data stores by augmenting their use of SQL~\cite{simitsis2009dbmss, IpeirotisZheng2025NLIDBUsers}.
In fact, the use of natural language to describe and execute relational data queries significantly reduces the onboarding time for novice users even in complex, highly relational data sets~\cite{fei2014, fei2016}.
In these examples natural language acts as a higher abstraction level over SQL, increasing the layers in the system without reducing expressivity.
Natural language improves the ability of a non-expert to express their intention, as long as there is a reliable translation layer to port this back into the query language~\cite{IpeirotisZheng2025NLIDBUsers}.

Users of this higher-level interface consistently outperform peers with the same experience attempting to complete tasks in SQL\@.
The time required to produce a working solution down-trends for NL interface users, indicating a meaningful reduction in cognitive load and barrier to entry; the abstraction is doing work that the user would otherwise have to perform themselves~\cite{IpeirotisZheng2025NLIDBUsers}.
The runtime quality of their solutions also improves, evidence that the translation layer contributes more than mere access; by converting user intention into executable logic, it enables computationally sensical optimisations that the user need not reason about explicitly~\cite{IpeirotisZheng2025NLIDBUsers}.
Comparatively, SQL users show flat or worsening performance on both measures, confirming that novices cannot achieve this level of optimisation through direct query authorship at first use.
Evidence further suggests that the higher abstraction level reduced the need for iterative correction; users arrived at better solutions upfront, with fewer reformulations required~\cite{IpeirotisZheng2025NLIDBUsers}.
Abstraction, good abstraction, results in a reduced lead time to success for new users.

Interestingly, comparative studies showed that for experts in SQL, the natural language interface can at times hinder their ability to effectively interact with the system~\cite{IpeirotisZheng2025NLIDBUsers}.
A takeaway here is that the effectiveness of an abstraction depends on its suitability for its audience; the quality of an abstraction should be measured against its ability to benefit its intended audience.

Abstraction also influences the way in which we interact with a problem space.
The natural language interface for data stores saw users prefer to reason semantically about the outcomes of their queries, that is to verify what they implement is what they intended rather than reason about the syntactic nature of their queries~\cite{IpeirotisZheng2025NLIDBUsers}.
It can shift the cognitive burden from deciding how to do something to deciding what it is one wants to do.
Abstraction, therefore, can have real-world impacts not only on how problems are solved but also how they are interpreted and perceived.
Novel ideation and problem-solving approaches can be promoted by removing the need to spend cognitive energy on determining how to translate intent to action.

The influence of tooling familiarity on adoption is further evidenced within the quantum computing domain itself.
An empirical survey of quantum practitioners found that Qiskit, a Python-embedded quantum framework, accounted for over sixty per cent of reported language use~\cite{unknown2024qplusage}.
The authors attribute this dominance in part to the fact that the majority of respondents were already experienced Python programmers — the barrier to entry is reduced when the tooling meets users at their existing level of competency~\cite{unknown2024qplusage}.

This is not merely a convenience argument.
It is evidence that the abstraction level of a tool, and its proximity to the concepts and interfaces a user already commands, is a practical determinant of what gets adopted.
An abstraction that demands practitioners to acquire an entirely new conceptual vocabulary before they can be productive will consistently lose to one that builds on existing fluency, even if the latter is technically inferior~\cite{unknown2024qplusage}.

Abstraction matters, but only insofar as it is well-designed; abstractions that fail to improve tractability for their intended users become barriers rather than tools.

Take, for example, a survey of over two hundred and fifty respondents working with quantum programming languages~\cite{unknown2024qplusage}.
The majority were experienced classical programmers (over 83\% had five or more years of professional experience), yet the same proportion had minimal quantum language experience.
Nearly half held computer science degrees, compared to 37\% from physics backgrounds.

Despite this, the dominant languages in use impose physics-oriented conceptual vocabularies: circuit semantics, gate-level operations and quantum-specific constructs that have no classical analogue~\cite{unknown2024qplusage}.
The result is a glossary burden; practitioners are required to acquire new conceptual vocabulary before they can be productive, one misaligned with the background most of them bring.
The survey confirms this: practitioners cited lack of conceptual quantum knowledge and challenging, inconsistent syntax as primary challenges, and over 31\% identified the absence of higher-level abstraction as a key missing feature~\cite{unknown2024qplusage}.
Mapping clarity compounds the problem; even the most popular framework, Qiskit, was noted by users as low-level and architecturally complex despite its familiarity advantage.
These findings point toward a specific standard for abstraction quality in this domain: abstractions must reduce glossary burden by aligning with existing intuitions and must improve mapping clarity so that practitioners can reason about their problem without being forced to reason about the machine.

In the current state of quantum computation, abstraction fails to provide sufficient tractability or advantages given the complexity of the domain.
Abstraction does not result in a problem space that is easier to reason about for novices in quantum mechanics~\cite{unknown2024qplusage}.
This will be discussed in more detail in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:in_quantum_computing}.

\subsection{Abstraction in quantum computing}\label{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:in_quantum_computing}
The current state of quantum computing systems is a spectrum of languages and frameworks; consistent with the abstraction hierarchy established in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:defining_abstraction}; spanning assembly-like languages such as OpenQASM~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}, embedded frameworks like Qiskit~\cite{aleksandrowicz2019qiskit} and high-level structured programming languages such as Silq~\cite{bichsel2020silq} and Qmod~\cite{vax2025qmodexpressivehighlevelquantum}.
Each layer progressively hides the complexity required to interact with it.
Embedded frameworks such as Qiskit enable a user to define quantum circuits without considering pulse or transition timings which would be required by OpenQASM specifications.
Silq and Qmod likewise provide methods of uncomputation that remove the need to directly implement reversal routines~\cite{bichsel2020silq, vax2025qmodexpressivehighlevelquantum}.

Formalisation of these abstractions, how they are defined and their role in quantum computing design has become a literary focus~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy,nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
An abstraction hierarchy for quantum computing has been suggested following the same \textbf{Programming}, \textbf{Execution} and \textbf{Hardware} layers as classical systems~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy} as shown in Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:abstraction:quantum_hierarchy}.

Abstract machines like the von Neumann random access memory (RAM) were critical in the successful development of productive classical systems.
Abstract machines provide a conceptual framework for defining the responsibilities of lower and higher level abstractions.
Candidate machines for quantum systems exist, such as quantum random access memory or the quantum Turing machine~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}
Their suitability as foundations for productive programming abstractions has not been established.
Criteria to assess their suitability has been suggested building on what makes classical abstract machines successful and what is required in general for productive programming with fifteen criteria established as shown in Table~\ref{tab:lit_rev:abstraction:qam_criteria}~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.

\begin{longtable}[htbp]{@{}p{7cm}p{7cm}@{}}
    \caption{Fifteen criteria for assessing the suitability and effectiveness of abstract machines for quantum computing~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.}
    \label{tab:lit_rev:abstraction:qam_criteria} \\
    \toprule
    \textbf{Criterion} & \textbf{Description} \\ \midrule
    1. Turing completeness & Can perform the same computations as any other classical or quantum machine; can simulate other machines and run any valid program without new features. \\
    2. Finite symbolic state & Available resources, symbols, memory, and states are finite and known; state can be decomposed into addressable subunits. \\
    3. Symbolic denotational syntax & Reasoning in terms of how operations impact system state (side effects) without needing to understand hardware execution; no circuit elements. \\
    4. Representation-independent data types & No reference to low-level, specific circuit elements (bits, qubits, etc.) for data types; data representation does not impact programming. \\
    5. Stable instruction set architecture & Instructions are finite, limited, and can express all programs; they remain constant across different hardware and are compact enough for humans to remember. \\
    6. Verifiable formal content & Operations are mathematically defined and independent of their specific hardware implementation. \\
    7. Compact instruction representation & The size of storing an instruction is constant and not influenced by the complexity of its hardware implementation. \\
    8. Classical-quantum regularity & Classical and quantum instructions coexist in the same machine at the same level of importance; neither requires hardware knowledge to reason about. \\
    9. Degeneracy of implementation & Building and running instructions does not violate physics; specific hardware is not required to preserve contracts between instructions or outcomes. \\
    10. Predictable procedural composability & Instructions can be combined into procedures with known outcomes and predictable system impact. \\
    11. Intrinsic ensemble semantics & Instructions can receive multiple inputs and produce multiple outputs; distribution data types are well-defined; the number of shots required is elided. \\
    12. Resource constructible functions & Time and space complexity can be reasoned about in abstract units rather than physical hardware realizations. \\
    13. Standard instruction cycle & Instructions execute within a single, mechanizable control loop dependent only on addressable units, not their internal workings. \\
    14. Classical control flow & Classical control structures can be implemented succinctly using the provided instruction set. \\
    15. Quantum/hybrid control flow & Quantum or hybrid control structures can be implemented succinctly using the instruction set. \\ \bottomrule
\end{longtable}

Quantum computing systems have made and continue to make progress towards higher levels of productive abstraction.
That progress has focused on improving the functional abstractions within the conceptual circuit model, providing easier ways to produce and describe quantum circuits; this represents an improvement to the operational surface of the circuit model rather than to its conceptual foundations.
The circuit model does not represent an abstract machine model capable of meaningful productivity~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines} and reduction in cognitive load for end users; qubits, gates, phases are all still required knowledge.

Improving the way in which programmers interact with the circuit model does not meaningfully progress towards a state of abstraction where \textit{intent} can be described using concepts shared by the programmer and machine~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy}.
Instead, the circuit model represents concepts defined by the machine and learned by the programmer and will fail to provide the necessary ``leap'' that would mirror the ``Cambrian explosion'' of languages and algorithms seen after the advent of RAM~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
That leap will only be possible when conceptual barriers to entry are eliminated by improved high-level abstractions as shown in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:why_matters}.

The hierarchy previously presented represents one of the first systematic attempts to define and critically examine abstraction within quantum computing, formally establishing what functionality is intended to be owned by different types of languages or frameworks.
Through that definition it is shown clearly that there is significant ``bleed through'' between these layers or leaky abstractions~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy} as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:lit_rev:abstraction:abstraction_bleed_through}.

The presence of leaky abstractions in higher level languages is further evidence that a productive or meaningful abstraction level has not yet been established.
It is not possible to operate solely within the programming layer because it leaks considerably into the execution layer.

Even the available abstract machines show consistent failures against the criteria most directly governing the programming layer~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Symbolic denotational syntax.
    \item Representation-independent data types.
    \item Stable instruction set architecture.
\end{itemize}
These criteria guarantee stable information hiding between programming interfaces and hardware implementations.
Their violation demonstrates that there is no established model for abstraction that can guarantee appropriate information hiding.

Positive progress has been made towards genuine information hiding abstractions in quantum computing.
Silq is among the first high-level quantum programming languages to introduce automatic uncomputation managed by a compiler~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
Unlike languages such as Q\# or Qmod which provide programmer led methods for marking the need for uncomputation~\cite{vax2025qmodexpressivehighlevelquantum} Silq uses its type system to guarantee situations requiring uncomputation can be identified and actioned by the compiler.
Automatic uncomputation could be seen as akin to the development of garbage collection in classical computing representing automated clean-up of allocated resources.

Silq and Qmod both offer strong type systems that show linearity and prevent physical violations such as no-cloning.
These features all represent improvements to the developer experience and a step towards information hiding and correctness guarantees we expect from higher abstraction levels.

Like garbage collection in classical computing, automatic uncomputation and improved type safety reduce programmer burden within the existing abstraction layer; they do not constitute a transition to a new conceptual space.
Silq, like Qiskit or Q\#, still uses qubits, gates, phases and the circuit as its primary conceptual programming model.

Advanced circuit development frameworks like Qiskit or Pennylane~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane} can be similarly judged.
They improve the experience of programming a circuit by providing embedded Python frameworks but still require the user to know about the circuit model.
These all represent leaky abstractions if they are considered as part of any kind of programming layer as defined by the hierarchy.

The systems and languages described here all serve a single conceptual audience, experts in physics or quantum mechanics; their abstractions serve to improve that audience's interactions with the system.
What they fail to offer is an abstraction that can increase the reach or audience beyond that domain; this problem is categorised throughout the literature~\cite{cobb2022higherlevelabstractions, unknown2024qplusage}.

Empirical evidence from practitioners reflects this directly: over 40\% believe too many quantum languages have already been developed, with 31\% explicitly identifying the need for higher-level languages and inadequate abstraction tooling as a primary challenge~\cite{unknown2024qplusage}.
Notably, the majority of quantum programming practitioners are experienced classical software engineers~\cite{unknown2024qplusage}; a community that already reasons fluently with high-level abstractions.
The barrier is not programming competence; it is the conceptual distance between the circuit model and the abstractions that the community is equipped to work with.
Continued iteration within the circuit model risks compounding that fatigue without addressing its cause.

Meaningful and productive abstraction in quantum computing is challenging, quantum computation represents a paradigmatic shift in how computational processes are perceived and designed~\cite{furntratt2024higherabstractionlevels}.
It is no longer possible to work with standard classical assumptions that underpin the way abstractions have historically been reasoned with, such as data representation being independent of its environment and being able to copy, pass and inspect data freely~\cite{LiskovGuttag1986, yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Superpositions cannot be inspected without losing information in the system, superpositions cannot be copied due to the no-cloning theorem.

Formal analysis establishes that arbitrary classical control flows cannot be implemented in superposition without violating unitarity~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Quantum languages must either restrict expressible control flows or introduce semantic models with no classical precedent~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Data represented as quantum states can be entangled with its environment, representations can be influenced by that entanglement; this cannot be reasoned with in traditional classical abstractions~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.

The quantum abstraction landscape shows genuine and sustained progress, but that progress is concentrated in the ergonomic boundary of the circuit model.
The programmer's required mental model has not fundamentally changed: quantum vocabulary remains mandatory, and the abstraction ceiling is consistently below what the classical criteria from Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:levels_elements} require.
This is not a failure of engineering effort; it reflects that quantum computation is structurally \textit{different}; it requires novel conceptual foundations for abstraction, not refinement of classical ones.

\subsection{Looking forward}\label{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:looking_forward}
The preceding subsections establish what abstraction is, what makes it effective and where quantum computing stands in relation to it.
Effective abstraction hides implementation detail, preserves semantic intention and reduces the cognitive burden on practitioners; evidence from classical systems demonstrates that when these properties are achieved, practitioners become more productive, arrive at better solutions faster and reason at the level of their problem rather than the level of the machine~\cite{Prechelt2000EmpiricalComparison,IpeirotisZheng2025NLIDBUsers}.
Quantum computing has made genuine progress toward this goal: layer separation has been formalised, type safety and automatic uncomputation have reduced certain classes of programmer error, hybrid execution models have improved interoperability with classical systems.
However, as Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:in_quantum_computing} demonstrates, this progress remains within the operational boundary of the circuit model; the programmer's conceptual vocabulary has not changed, and the abstraction ceiling is constrained by structural properties of quantum mechanics that the classical abstraction framework was not designed to accommodate.

The classical compiler chain illustrates the missing piece.
As established in Subsection~\ref{subsec:lit_rev:abstraction:levels_elements}, mature classical systems place a general-purpose source layer; languages like C and Java; above the assembly and hardware layers.
That source layer is hardware-independent, formally defined and expressive enough to allow practitioners to describe problems in their domain without knowledge of the machine executing the solution.
Quantum computing does not yet have an equivalent to that layer.OpenQASM and pulse-level languages sit at the assembly tier; Silq, Q\# and Qmod are positioned above them, but all three still require the programmer to reason in qubits, gates, and circuits: concepts defined by the machine, not the problem.
A C programmer does not need to know the instruction set of the processor executing their code; a Silq programmer cannot avoid reasoning about the circuit model.
The gap is not that quantum lacks a domain-level abstraction comparable to SQL or Python; it is that it lacks the source-layer foundation that classical computing built those domain abstractions on top of.
