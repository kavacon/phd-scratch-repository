\section{System assessments}\label{sec:system_assessments}
No system in the review set satisfies the litmus test in Section~\ref{subsubsec:taxonomy:quantum_implicit}; the quantum-implicit tier is unoccupied.
This section documents the scored profiles from which that finding derives.
The rubric defined in Table~\ref{tab:evaluation_rubric:scoring_levels}is applied to the ten primary systems selected in Sections~\ref{sec:taxonomy} and~\ref{sec:methodology}.
Each system receives axis scores A--E, a spectrum score (arithmetic mean with observed range), a headline tier level and a quantum bias count.
Per-system scorecards are in Section~\ref{subsec:system_assessments:system_scorecards}.
Comparative analysis is in Section~\ref{subsec:system_assessments:comparative}.
Quipper is excluded from the formal assessment;
Subsection~\ref{subsec:system_assessments:quipper_aside} includes it as a labelled reference point.
Findings are reported here; interpretation of RQ1 and RQ2 is in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\begin{description}

    Score~1 requires vocabulary targeting compiler and tooling authors.
    OpenQASM~3.0's vocabulary consists of named gates and qubit registers regardless of whether the representation is human-authored or compiler-generated.

    \item[B\,(0):] The primary artefact is an explicit gate sequence with classical control flow; version~3.0 extends this with timing constructs and pulse-level definitions at the same authoring level~\cite{cross2022openqasm3}.

The gate-level vocabulary of the representation places axes A, B, C and E at score~0.
All six hard-fail conditions are triggered.
The authoring surface directly exposes gate sequences as the primary artefact, named qubit allocation, mandatory quantum/classical declarations, interference-dependent gate selection, hardware timing constraints and pulse-level definitions.
Score~1 on the remaining axes would require a representation whose vocabulary and artefact format target compiler authors rather than gate-level programming.
OpenQASM~3.0's named-gate vocabulary does not meet this criterion regardless of authoring context.

The axis-A ceiling is the primary constraint; raising it to score~3 would require removing mandatory quantum type declarations and Dirac notation from typical initialisation workflows.
The axis-C and axis-D scores of~3 depend critically on a single preprint~\cite{reversiblelifetime2026} for the reversible lifetime semantics mechanism; this dependency is flagged as a leave-one-out case in Section~\ref{subsec:methodology:sensitivity}.

\subsection{Comparative analysis}\label{subsec:system_assessments:comparative}
Tier groupings throughout this subsection refer to the headline score assigned by the gating rule in Table~\ref{tab:evaluation_rubric:scoring_levels}.
Four systems receive a headline score of~2 and are grouped at the Embedded tier: Qiskit, PennyLane, Q\# and Qmod.

\subsubsection{Axis pattern analysis}\label{subsubsec:system_assessments:axis_patterns}
Axis scores increase with headline tier level on all five dimensions, but the degree of within-tier consistency varies by axis.

Axis~A (mental model and vocabulary) shows the tightest constraint across the review set.
Assembly systems score~0 and IR systems score~1.
All four Embedded systems score~2; two of three High-level systems also score~2, with only Quff reaching score~3.
No system reaches score~4 on axis~A, making it the most constrained axis in the assessment.

Axis~B (primary artefact) shows the most within-tier differentiation in the Embedded group: Qiskit and PennyLane score~2 while Q\# and Qmod score~3 through program-structured authoring surfaces.
All High-level systems score~3 on axis~B.
Score~4, defined as a mathematical or computational-level primary artefact without quantum vocabulary, is not reached by any system.

Axis~C (safety discipline and guarantees) follows the most consistent tier-progression pattern in the review set.
All Assembly systems score~0, both IR systems score~1, all four Embedded systems score~2 and all three High-level systems score~3.
No within-tier outliers appear on axis~C, making it the axis most closely aligned with the tier hierarchy.

Axis~D (compilation responsibility) follows a similar progression with one deviation.
OpenQASM~3.0 scores~1 on axis~D, above its headline Assembly tier, reflecting its use as a lowering target for embedded frameworks.
All High-level systems score~3; Qmod also scores~3 in the Embedded group through its synthesis engine.

Axis~E (execution and hardware adjacency) distinguishes Q\# from other Embedded systems.
Q\# reaches score~3 alongside the three High-level systems while Qiskit, PennyLane and Qmod score~2.
This shows that hardware agnosticism is not exclusively a feature of the High-level tier.

The comparison of headline score with spectrum score reveals an additional pattern.
Systems with uniform axis scores within their tier — Qiskit, PennyLane, QIR and Q-MLIR — have headline and spectrum scores that align exactly.
Q\# and Qmod have spectrum scores of~2.4 despite headline scores of~2; two axes in each case reach score~3 without meeting the four-axis threshold for headline~3.
Silq and Qutes have spectrum scores of~2.8 with headline scores of~3; axis~A at score~2 pulls the mean below the headline without affecting tier assignment.
These gaps identify systems where individual axes reach higher-tier positioning without achieving the consistency required for a higher headline score.

\subsubsection{Quantum bias drivers}\label{subsubsec:system_assessments:qb_drivers}
Quantum bias counts range from~3 (Quff) to~7 (Q-MLIR and PennyLane) across the review set.
The three High-level systems have the lowest counts (3--4), consistent with the expected reduction in required quantum concepts at higher tier levels.

Q-MLIR's count of~7 reflects the full IR-level concept set, including entanglement as a resource required for correct understanding of multi-qubit dialect operations.
PennyLane's count of~7 reflects the variational optimisation context.
Phase kickback, basis change, interference and entanglement are all required for correct gradient-based optimisation workflows, beyond what basic circuit construction demands.

Quantum bias count does not decrease monotonically from Assembly to Embedded.
OpenQASM (Assembly, quantum bias count~=~6), QIR (IR, quantum bias count~=~6) and Q\# (Embedded, quantum bias count~=~6) share the same count despite spanning three tiers.
The items differ: OpenQASM includes interference amplitude; Q\# includes reversibility and uncomputation required for correct use of the Adjoint functor; QIR includes hardware constraints and reversibility at the IR level.

Axis~B scores correlate with quantum bias reduction: systems whose primary artefact is a structured program consistently show lower quantum bias counts.
Q\# and Qmod retain counts of~6 and~5 respectively despite scoring~3 on axis~B, driven by qubit-level and amplitude-level vocabulary requirements that persist alongside the program-level authoring surface.
