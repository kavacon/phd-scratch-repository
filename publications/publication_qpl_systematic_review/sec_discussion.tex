\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
Ten quantum programming systems were assessed across five abstraction tiers using the rubric defined in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_rubric}.
No system in the review set occupies the quantum-implicit tier; the highest-scoring system reaches headline tier~3 uniformly across all five axes.
Quantum bias counts remain non-trivial even at the highest tier, indicating persistent substrate-level vocabulary requirements throughout the current landscape.
This section answers RQ1 and RQ2 in turn, names the quantum-implicit gap as a novel empirical contribution and draws design implications grounded in the assessment findings.

\subsection{RQ1: Current abstraction levels and the quantum-implicit gap}\label{subsec:discussion:rq1}
The current quantum programming landscape is structured across four populated tiers: Assembly, IR, Framework and High-level.
Assessment against the abstraction requirements prior work identifies for productive quantum programming reveals a graduated picture.
Lower-tier systems meet the requirements of their respective tiers and high-level systems partially meet usability requirements.
No system meets the requirements for the quantum-implicit tier; the tier at which programmers write non-trivial programs without quantum-mechanical prerequisites remains unoccupied~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy,nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.

The tier hierarchy is populated and each tier performs its designed function.
Assembly and IR systems meet the hardware-fidelity and compilation requirements documented in Section~\ref{sec:system_assessments}.
Frameworks lower the circuit-construction barrier through API abstraction and transpilation infrastructure.
The measured vocabulary burden remains substantial because the primary artefact is still a circuit; this reflects the tier's function, not a design failure~\cite{corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness,furntratt2024higherabstractionlevels}.
High-level systems partially meet the usability requirements established in Section~\ref{sec:background}~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy,nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.
The most capable assessed systems provide program-level primary artefacts, compiler-managed resource handling and hardware agnosticism; quantum vocabulary remains required at the programming boundary.
The quantum-implicit tier, which the literature identifies as the minimum for broad developer accessibility, is unoccupied.

Every high-level system except Quff scores~2 on axis~A (mental model and vocabulary) despite reaching score~3 on axes B--E; Quff reaches score~3 uniformly but score~4 on no axis.
The ceiling on axis~A appears across all assessed systems, including those with the most sophisticated safety disciplines and compilation models.
The vocabulary requirement at the programming boundary reflects a paradigm property of current quantum programming, not a feature gap.
Circuit-level programming requires bespoke quantum operations for each task in a manner the literature has likened to computation before abstraction layers became available~\cite{cobb2022higherlevelabstractions}.

The spectrum score surfaces within-tier differentiation that the headline score alone does not capture.
Headline score is a gated classification: a system attains tier~$S$ only if at least four of five axes score $\geq S$.

Accordingly, Q\# and Qmod should be read as mixed-profile systems: both exceed Framework-tier baselines on specific axes (reflected in their spectrum scores), but neither meets the four-axis threshold required for headline tier~3.
Q\# and Qmod score above their framework-tier peers on individual axes; both record a spectrum score of~2.4 against Qiskit and PennyLane at~2.0.
Within-tier differentiation of this kind is analogous to classical programming.

C, Java and Rust are each classified within the same tier, yet they occupy measurably distinct positions within it.
The quantum-implicit tier need not be perfectly implicit to provide a step change in accessibility.
A language that hides quantum vocabulary by default while retaining expert access is a sufficient target; Java manages memory by default while preserving low-level access as an explicit choice.

The near-perfect tier alignment also reveals a terminological issue.
The term \textit{high-level} in quantum computing has come to denote function-structured programming: any system in which programs are organised as named callables.
This does not carry the programmer-usability dimension the same term has in classical computing, where \textit{high-level} implies vocabulary-distance from the hardware substrate.
The Q\# and Qmod headline scores make this concrete.
Both systems are function-structured and provide substantial compiler assistance; neither achieves the consistent programmer-facing abstraction the rubric requires for headline~3.

Q\# is a compiled, statically typed language with its own operation semantics rather than an API layer over circuit primitives hosted in a classical language.
Its identical headline score to Framework-tier systems demonstrates that the abstraction ceiling is in the underlying data model, not the surface representation.

The near-perfect alignment between assessed and declared tiers might appear to indicate definitions calibrated to the assessed systems.
Three findings are inconsistent with this interpretation.
Q\# and Qmod score at headline~2, below their declared type; a rubric calibrated to confirmed declared types would not produce this downgrade.
Quantum bias counts do not decrease monotonically with declared tier: PennyLane, a framework-tier system, has a higher quantum bias count than either IR system or the Assembly system.
A Haskell-embedded functional framework scores at the same tier as Python-embedded imperative frameworks on abstraction axes, confirming the instrument measures abstraction properties rather than host language or paradigm.
The pre-registration of the review protocol prior to any scoring provides procedural protection against confirmation bias~\cite{kavadias2026qplSurveyProtocol}.
The three findings above provide corroborating empirical evidence.

\subsection{RQ2: A new quantum programming language is justified}\label{subsec:discussion:rq2}
The evidence supports the need for a new quantum programming language designed at a higher abstraction level.
The argument is paradigm-level, not feature-level.
No existing system fails because it lacks a specific feature that another system has proved possible; existing systems share a descriptive substrate that constrains how far abstraction can reach.

The structural analysis in Section~\ref{sec:background} establishes that the classical data structure contracts underlying higher-level language design do not transfer to the quantum setting~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
Classical programming assumes that data can be copied freely, that inspecting it has no side effects and that temporary results need not be reversed.
Each assumption fails for quantum data: no-cloning prohibits duplication, measurement collapses state on inspection and uncomputation introduces a mandatory reversal obligation without a classical equivalent.
A language built on these constraints can abstract their management but cannot abstract away the constraints themselves without a different foundational data model.

The scoring data confirms this mechanism at the programming surface.
Q\# and Qmod reach score~3 on axis~B, indicating that program-structured authoring is achievable within the current paradigm.
Both systems remain at score~2 on axis~A because the program's type system inherits the vocabulary of the circuit model it is built on; a function form does not change the underlying data representation.

Q\# makes this concrete.
Unlike Framework-tier systems, Q\# is not an API layer over circuit primitives hosted in a classical language; it is a compiled language with its own type system, operation semantics and static analysis guarantees.
Its surface architecture is more distant from the typical Framework approach than any other system at headline~2.
The identical headline score demonstrates that the source of the abstraction ceiling is not the surface representation but the underlying compilation target.
Whether a programmer writes quantum operations as Python function calls or as statically typed callables in a compiled language, the circuit model remains the data representation the program describes.
Changing the surface representation does not change what the programmer must hold in mind.

Axis~C provides a structural parallel.
Axis~C follows a perfect tier-progression across the review set, from score~0 at Assembly to score~3 at High-level.
Each tier transition was enabled by a type-theoretic advance.
API-level discipline enabled the move from Assembly to Framework; linear and affine type systems enabled the move to High-level~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
Score~4 requires safety guarantees framed in non-quantum terms, with quantum operations accessible only as escape hatches.
A type system that starts from quantum state and enforces constraints over it cannot reach this criterion by extension~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.

The quantum bias count distribution supports the same conclusion from a different direction.
PennyLane, a framework-tier system, has a higher quantum bias count than either IR system or the Assembly system.
Quantum bias is use-case-driven, not tier-driven; variational optimisation requires interference, phase kickback and entanglement reasoning that basic circuit construction does not~\cite{corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness}.
A quantum-implicit language must provide a use-case model in which these concepts do not arise as programmer responsibilities, not merely hide them behind an API.

The quantum bias count at the highest-scoring tier provides the empirical anchor.
Quff has a quantum bias count of~3: qubits as a distinct resource, measurement collapse and no-cloning and linearity.
These are the three foundational quantum mechanical constraints the structural analysis in Section~\ref{sec:background} identifies as irreducible at the circuit model boundary~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
No programmer using any current system avoids all three; the three concepts are intrinsic to the circuit model, not incidental to it.
A language that removes all three from the programmer's required vocabulary is not an extension of any current system; it requires a new foundational model of quantum data and computation~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.

\subsection{The quantum-implicit gap as a novel contribution}\label{subsec:discussion:contribution}
The quantum-implicit tier is the central novel contribution of this review's taxonomy.
Prior comparative surveys classified quantum programming systems by design orientation, paradigm or abstraction level, but none defined the quantum-implicit tier as a named category with explicit entry criteria~\cite{Gay2006QPLSurvey,furntratt2024higherabstractionlevels}.
This review provides the first structured definition of the tier.
The tier is definable in criterion terms, its requirements are derivable from the literature~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy,nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines,yuan2025foundationalabstractions} and no current system occupies it.

Prior taxonomy established a useful vocabulary for the field.
The earliest systematic survey classified quantum programming languages by design paradigm: imperative, functional and formal semantic approaches~\cite{Gay2006QPLSurvey}.
This provided a framework for describing what systems exist but did not define tier membership by programmer experience or vocabulary requirement.
Subsequent comparative work identified fragmentation and stalled abstraction progress but did not define the quantum-implicit tier as a named, measurable category~\cite{furntratt2024higherabstractionlevels,corralesgarro2025productivityexpressiveness}.
What this review adds is a criterion-based definition.
The definition is grounded in the abstraction requirements identified in Section~\ref{sec:background} and operationalised as a five-axis rubric with explicit score-4 entry conditions.

The measurement instrument provides a secondary contribution beyond tier classification.
The spectrum score captures within-tier quality in a way that prior taxonomy work did not.
Systems at the same headline tier occupy measurably different positions: Q\# and Qmod record spectrum scores of~2.4 while Qiskit and PennyLane record~2.0, all four at headline~2.
The Q\# and Qmod headline scores are not a criticism of those systems; they reveal that function-structured programming is a necessary but not sufficient condition for the high-level tier.
The instrument provides a vocabulary for this distinction that the field's informal classification does not.

The contribution is to identify and characterise the quantum-implicit gap, not to fill it.
A gap that has not been defined and measured cannot be systematically closed; the definition and empirical validation here are a necessary precondition for any future language design effort targeting this tier.
The design implications this finding supports are drawn in Section~\ref{subsec:discussion:implications}, and the programme of work the gap motivates is addressed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.

\subsection{Design implications}\label{subsec:discussion:implications}
The assessment findings support four design implications for a quantum-implicit programming language.
Each is grounded in axis evidence from Section~\ref{sec:system_assessments} and makes contact with the abstract machine requirements identified in Section~\ref{sec:background}~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.

\paragraph{Quantum data model first}
No assessed system reaches score~4 on axis~D.
Four systems reach score~3, providing quantum-typed data structures with generic backend representations.
The step from score~3 to score~4 requires a data model in which quantum operations are expressed as transformations over abstract data types rather than as circuit primitives~\cite{DiMatteo2024AbstractionHierarchy}.
The instruction set should be designed against this data representation, not the reverse.
Languages that begin with an instruction set and add type safety afterwards cannot avoid encoding circuit vocabulary in their type signatures.

\paragraph{Computation model separation}
Axis~C follows a step-function progression driven by successive type-theoretic advances: from untyped gate sequences to qubit-lifetime type checks to linear and affine type systems~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
Each tier transition on this axis was a theoretical advance, not an engineering one.
Score~4 requires safety guarantees expressed without reference to quantum state at the type level.
A type theory whose primitives are circuit objects cannot produce these guarantees by extension~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
The computation model of a quantum-implicit language must be defined over the abstract data representation, not imposed as a constraint system on circuit-level operations.

\paragraph{Measurement as system policy}
Quff's quantum bias count of~3 identifies the empirical minimum vocabulary at headline tier~3: qubits as a distinct resource, measurement collapse and no-cloning and linearity.
All three are irreducible at the circuit model boundary~\cite{yuan2025foundationalabstractions}.
The closest existing evidence of progress beyond this boundary is compiler-managed uncomputation, which removes the ancilla reversal obligation from the programmer's working model~\cite{bichsel2020silq}.
A quantum-implicit language must extend this pattern to measurement.
Making the observation boundary a system-level policy requires a computation model in which measurement outcomes are consumed by the program structure, not returned to the programmer for explicit inspection~\cite{nunez_corrales2025betterabstractmachines}.

\paragraph{Vocabulary-free by default, expert access preserved}
A quantum-implicit language need not eliminate quantum vocabulary entirely.
Practitioner evidence indicates that the primary barrier for the majority of current users is the requirement to use quantum vocabulary in common-case programming, not in advanced or performance-critical paths~\cite{ferreira2024qplusage}.
The appropriate design target is a language in which programs can be written, tested and reasoned about without quantum-mechanical prerequisites in the common case, with explicit vocabulary available for optimisation, verification and low-level access.
This is the established approach in safe-default language design, where expert access is preserved as an opt-in rather than required as a default.
